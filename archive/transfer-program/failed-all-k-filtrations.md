# The quadratic remainder algebra is not the transfer tangent cone

> **Archived refutation.**  This note is retained as the decisive failure
> record for the former all-`k` transfer programme.  It is not an active
> dependency.  See [README.md](README.md).

## Status and conclusion

**Counterexample (proved).**  The proposed identification of the transverse
associated graded of the completed monic square/cube factorization ring with

\[
 \mathcal C_k=
 \mathbb Q[s_1,\ldots,s_k,a_0,\ldots,a_{k-1}]/
 \bigl(\operatorname{coeff}_Z\operatorname{rem}_S(A^2)\bigr)
\]

is false already for `k=2`.  The degree-two components of the displayed
coefficient equations generate the quadratic remainder ideal, but some
coefficient equations have zero degree-two component and a nonzero cubic
initial form.  Further initial relations can also arise from cancellations
among generators.  At `k=2` the missing relation is `X^3`.

Moreover, `C_k` is not finite over the `S`-base for any `k>=2`.  Its fiber at
`S=Z^k` has dimension `floor(k/2)`, hence infinite vector-space length.  It
therefore cannot be finite flat of rank `2^k`.  This refutes the proposed
filtration mechanism; it does **not** refute finite flatness of the actual
square/cube transfer ring, whose exact low-degree fibers remain examples.

All rings below are over `Q`.  The divisions by `2`, `8`, `9`, `48`, and
`64` make characteristic zero (or suitable explicit inversions) part of the
square/cube presentation.  No positive-characteristic statement is made.

## 1. Exact presentation and transverse filtration

Let

\[
 B=\mathbb Q[s_1,\ldots,s_k],\qquad
 S=Z^k+s_1Z^{k-1}+\cdots+s_k,
 \qquad A=\sum_{i=0}^{k-1}a_iZ^i.
\]

Divide by the monic polynomial `S`:

\[
 A^2=SQ+R,\qquad \deg_ZR<k,
\]

and put

\[
 \mathcal F_k=9Q^2+8SAQ-48S^2R-64AR.                 \tag{1}
\]

Let `P=B[a_0,...,a_(k-1)]`, let `I_k` be generated by all coefficients in
`Z` of (1), and let `j=(a_0,...,a_(k-1))`.  For the transverse completion
`T_hat=(P/I_k)^`, completed `j`-adically, define

\[
 F^d\widehat T_k=j^d\widehat T_k,
 \qquad
 \operatorname{gr}_j\widehat T_k
 =\bigoplus_{d\ge0}F^d\widehat T_k/F^{d+1}\widehat T_k. \tag{2}
\]

Equivalently one may first replace `B` by `Lambda=Q[[s_1,...,s_k]]` and work
in `Lambda[[a_0,...,a_(k-1)]]`.  Noetherian adic completion is exact on the
finite quotients needed in each graded degree, so (2) is

\[
 \operatorname{gr}_j\widehat T_k
 \simeq B[a_0,\ldots,a_{k-1}]/\operatorname{in}_j(I_k), \tag{3}
\]

with `B` replaced by `Lambda` in the completed-base convention.  Here
`in_j(f)` is the summand of least total `a`-degree of `f`, and
`in_j(I_k)` is generated by `in_j(f)` for **all** `f in I_k`, not merely for
the displayed generators.

This last distinction is the source of the counterexample.

## 2. What the raw equations actually imply

Both `Q` and `R` are homogeneous quadratic polynomials in the transverse
variables.  Thus the transverse degrees of the four terms of (1) are

| term | transverse degree |
|---|---:|
| `-48 S^2 R` | 2 |
| `8 S A Q` | 3 |
| `-64 A R` | 3 |
| `9 Q^2` | 4 |

Consequently the degree-two component of the coefficient vector is the
coefficient vector of `-48S^2R`.  Multiplication by the monic polynomial
`S^2` gives a triangular, unit-diagonal transformation on the coefficient
vector of `R`: starting with the highest coefficient, one recovers every
coefficient of `R` successively.  Hence

\[
 \bigl((\operatorname{coeff}_Z\mathcal F_k)_{\text{degree }2}\bigr)
 =\bigl(\operatorname{coeff}_ZR\bigr).                \tag{4}
\]

Equation (4) does not say that every coefficient equation has transverse
order two.  Where its degree-two component vanishes, its initial form may
have degree three or four.  Even after all such initial forms are included,
in general

\[
 (\operatorname{in}_j f_1,\ldots,\operatorname{in}_j f_N)
 \subsetneq \operatorname{in}_j(f_1,\ldots,f_N),      \tag{5}
\]

because combinations cancelling their lowest terms can create further
initial relations.  Treating (4) as equality in (3) was the invalid step.

## 3. Decisive counterexample at `k=2`

Write

\[
 S=Z^2+s_1Z+s_2,\qquad A=XZ+Y.
\]

Then

\[
 Q=X^2,qquad
 R=(2XY-s_1X^2)Z+(Y^2-s_2X^2).
\]

Direct coefficient elimination in (1) gives the exact transfer ideal

\[
 I_2=(X^3,\ 2XY-s_1X^2,\ Y^2-s_2X^2).                \tag{6}
\]

This equality is checked by reductions in both directions over
`Q[s_1,s_2]` in the accompanying script.  Each relation in (6) is
homogeneous for transverse degree.  Therefore

\[
 \operatorname{gr}_j\widehat T_2
 =\frac{\mathbb Q[[s_1,s_2]][X,Y]}
 {(X^3,\ 2XY-s_1X^2,\ Y^2-s_2X^2)}.                  \tag{7}
\]

The proposed quadratic algebra omits the first relation:

\[
 \mathcal C_2=
 \frac{\mathbb Q[s_1,s_2,X,Y]}
 {(2XY-s_1X^2,\ Y^2-s_2X^2)}.                        \tag{8}
\]

At `s_1=s_2=0`, (7) has basis

\[
 1,\ X,\ Y,\ X^2,
\]

whereas (8) specializes to

\[
 \mathbb Q[X,Y]/(XY,Y^2),                            \tag{9}
\]

in which every power of `X` survives.  Thus (8) is positive-dimensional,
while a tangent cone of the finite local algebra (7) has finite length.

There is also a universal torsion certificate.  With
`Delta=s_1^2-4s_2`, the two relations in (8) imply

\[
 \Delta X^3=0,
\]

but `X^3` is nonzero, as its specialization in (9) is nonzero.  The missing
initial relation is exactly what removes this discriminant-supported
torsion in the true `k=2` transfer algebra.

## 4. The universal quadratic remainder algebra

Let

\[
 \mathcal C_k=B[a_0,\ldots,a_{k-1}]/(\operatorname{coeff}_ZR).
\]

On the squarefree locus of `S`, pass to an etale splitting algebra and let
`rho_1,...,rho_k` be its roots.  Evaluation identifies `B[Z]/(S)` with a
product of `k` copies of the splitting algebra.  If `x_i=A(rho_i)`, the
equation `A^2=0 mod S` becomes

\[
 x_1^2=\cdots=x_k^2=0.
\]

Hence `C_k` is finite flat of rank `2^k` on the squarefree locus.  This proves
its generic rank, but says nothing about the discriminant fiber.

At the maximally repeated polynomial `S=Z^k`, put

\[
 D_k=\mathbb Q[a_0,\ldots,a_{k-1}]/I_k^{\rm conv},
 \qquad
 I_k^{\rm conv}=(c_0,\ldots,c_{k-1}),
\]

where

\[
 c_n=\sum_{i+j=n}a_i a_j.                              \tag{10}
\]

These are precisely the first `k` coefficients of `A(Z)^2`.

### Theorem 4.1 (all `k`: support and dimension)

For every `k>=1`, set `h=ceil(k/2)`.  Then

\[
 \sqrt{I_k^{\rm conv}}=(a_0,\ldots,a_{h-1}).           \tag{11}
\]

It is the unique minimal prime.  Consequently

\[
 \dim D_k=k-h=\lfloor k/2\rfloor.                     \tag{12}
\]

For `k=1`, `D_1=Q[a_0]/(a_0^2)` has length two.  For every `k>=2`, `D_k`
has infinite `Q`-vector-space length.

#### Proof

Work first at a geometric point.  If `j` is the least index for which
`a_j` is nonzero, then the coefficient of `Z^(2j)` in `A^2` is `a_j^2`.
The equations (10) therefore force `2j>=k`, hence
`a_0=...=a_(h-1)=0`.  Conversely those vanishings make every coefficient of
degree below `k` in `A^2` zero.  Nullstellensatz over `Qbar`, followed by
faithfully flat descent to `Q`, proves (11).  The remaining assertions
follow immediately.  In particular, setting the first `h` variables to zero
exhibits the polynomial quotient `Q[a_h,...,a_(k-1)]`.  QED

### Corollary 4.2 (not a complete intersection)

For `k>=2`, the `k` quadrics (10) do not form a regular sequence.  Indeed,
their ideal has height `ceil(k/2)<k`.  For `k=1`, the single quadric
`a_0^2` is a regular sequence in `Q[a_0]`.

The nonzero square-zero elements at a repeated root are not harmless
nilpotents in a zero-dimensional complete intersection.  Every polynomial

\[
 A=Z^{\lceil k/2\rceil}B(Z),\qquad \deg A<k,
\]

satisfies `A^2=0 mod Z^k`, and the coefficients of `B` vary freely.  Thus,
for every `k>=2`, the quadrics have projective common zeros and an affine
cone of dimension (12).  The
zero-dimensional hypothesis needed for a Bezout length `2^k` is false.

### Embedded behavior already at `k=2`

For `k=2`,

\[
 I_2^{\rm conv}=(a_0^2,2a_0a_1)=a_0(a_0,a_1).
\]

It has minimal prime `(a_0)` and embedded associated prime `(a_0,a_1)`.
Thus an embedded component appears in the first nontrivial case.

## 5. Hilbert series and depth: exact bounded data

The standard grading gives every `a_i` degree one.  The all-`k` conclusions
needed for the counterexample are (11)--(12), infinite length, and failure
of regularity.  No closed all-`k` Hilbert-series or depth formula is claimed
here.  The following values are exact Groebner computations, deliberately
bounded by `k<=6`:

| `k` | `dim D_k` | Hilbert series `H_k(t)` | depth at the homogeneous maximal ideal |
|---:|---:|---|---:|
| 1 | 0 | `1+t` | 0 |
| 2 | 1 | `(1+t-t^2)/(1-t)` | 0 |
| 3 | 1 | `(1+2t)/(1-t)` | 1 |
| 4 | 2 | `(1+2t-t^2-t^3)/(1-t)^2` | 1 |
| 5 | 2 | `(1+3t+t^2-t^3)/(1-t)^2` | 1 |
| 6 | 3 | `(1+3t-3t^3)/(1-t)^3` | 2 |

For fixed `k` these are exact results, not numerical sampling.  They are not
an induction.  In particular, the negative coefficients in the reduced
Hilbert numerators for `k=4,5,6` rule out Cohen--Macaulayness there.  The
depth values follow from exact successive linear nonzerodivisor tests and
the dimension bound.  Extending the table does not repair the failed
associated-graded claim and is not proposed as a research direction.

## 6. Why the proposed flatness strategies stop

| proposed mechanism | outcome for `C_k` |
|---|---|
| Cohen--Macaulay plus constant fiber length | the repeated-root fiber is positive-dimensional for `k>=2` |
| miracle flatness | finiteness and equidimensionality hypotheses fail |
| uniform Groebner degeneration | it must include higher initial relations; the remainder quadrics alone are insufficient |
| primary factorization of `S` | a primary factor `Z^k` creates the free family in Theorem 4.1 |
| Rees algebra | the correct Rees special fiber uses `in_j(I_k)`, not the initial forms of a chosen generating list |
| filtered lifting | its proposed associated graded is false, so there is no basis to lift from `C_k` |

Thus none of these methods can prove finite flatness of `C_k`.  A future
proof of finite flatness for the *actual* transfer ring would have to explain
uniformly the additional initial relations, beginning with `X^3` at `k=2`.
