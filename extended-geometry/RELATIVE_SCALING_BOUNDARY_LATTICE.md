# Relative scaling and the boundary lattice

This note consolidates the differential, normalization, unit, and Picard
calculations for factorization constructions.  They are four forms of one
integer lattice map.  It also records two consequences which are easy to
miss when the calculations are made separately: every natural two-factor
complement has trivial canonical bundle, while every natural ordered
factorization complement with at least three factors has a nonconstant unit.

Work over a field `k`.  Statements involving ordinary finite etale groups may
be read in characteristic zero.  The group-scheme formulations remain valid
in arbitrary characteristic with the qualifications stated below.

## 1. The two-factor weight map

Let `A` and `B` be nonzero binary forms of positive degrees `p` and `q`.  Put

\[
 r=\operatorname{Res}(A,B)
\]

and let `m` be a nonzero linear functional of the product `AB`.  Under

\[
 (A,B)\longmapsto(\lambda A,\mu B),
\]

the two functions transform by

\[
 (r,m)\longmapsto
 (\lambda^q\mu^p r,\lambda\mu m).                    \tag{1}
\]

Thus the governing homomorphism of scaling tori is

\[
 \Phi_{p,q}:\mathbb G_m^2\longrightarrow\mathbb G_m^2,
 \qquad
 (\lambda,\mu)\longmapsto
 (\lambda^q\mu^p,\lambda\mu),                       \tag{2}
\]

with weight matrix

\[
 W_{p,q}=\begin{pmatrix}q&p\\1&1\end{pmatrix},
 \qquad \det W_{p,q}=q-p.                            \tag{3}
\]

The transpose lattice map will reappear as the boundary-class map.

## 2. Differential and normalization theorem

Consider

\[
 \Theta_{p,q}(A,B)=
 \bigl(AB,\operatorname{Res}(A,B)\bigr).             \tag{4}
\]

### Proposition 2.1

On the coprime locus, `Theta_(p,q)` is etale exactly when `q-p` is nonzero in
`k`.

### Proof

If a tangent vector `(dot A,dot B)` is killed by the differential of
multiplication, then

\[
 \dot A B+A\dot B=0.
\]

Coprimality and the degree bounds imply

\[
 (\dot A,\dot B)=c(A,-B).
\]

The resultant has coefficient bidegree `(q,p)`, so

\[
 d\operatorname{Res}_{(A,B)}(A,-B)
 =(q-p)\operatorname{Res}(A,B).                      \tag{5}
\]

The source and target of (4) have the same dimension.  Hence its differential
is invertible precisely under the stated condition.  In characteristic zero
this is equivalent to `p!=q`; in characteristic `ell` one must also require
`ell` not to divide `q-p`.  \(\square\)

Now suppose `r*m!=0`.  Normalizing both functions to one requires

\[
 \lambda^q\mu^p r=1,
 \qquad
 \lambda\mu m=1.
\]

Eliminating `mu` gives

\[
 \boxed{\lambda^{q-p}=\frac{m^p}{r}},
 \qquad
 \mu=\frac1{\lambda m}.                              \tag{6}
\]

In particular, the right side of the first formula is `m^p/r`, not its
inverse.

### Proposition 2.2

Put `d=|q-p|` and assume `d>0`.  The normalized representatives form a torsor
under

\[
 \ker\Phi_{p,q}=\{(\zeta,\zeta^{-1}):\zeta^d=1\}
 \simeq\mu_d.                                        \tag{7}
\]

Consequently normalization is unique exactly when `d=1`.  If `d>1`, it is a
finite etale ambiguity when `d` is invertible in `k`; if the characteristic
divides `d`, the same kernel is the non-etale group scheme `mu_d`.

When `p=q`, the ratio

\[
 \frac r{m^p}                                         \tag{8}
\]

is invariant under the full scaling torus.  Simultaneous normalization to
`r=m=1` is possible only on its value-one fiber, and the relative
`G_m`-kernel survives there.

## 3. Boundary units, Picard group, and the canonical torsor

Let

\[
 X=\mathbb P^p\times\mathbb P^q,
 \qquad
 U=X\setminus(R\cup E),                              \tag{9}
\]

where `R={r=0}` and `E={m=0}`.  The resultant divisor is prime and

\[
 [R]=(q,p),\qquad [E]=(1,1)                          \tag{10}
\]

in `Pic(X)=Z^2`.  For the next statement assume that `E` is also prime.  If
`E` is reducible, localization must instead use all of its prime components
and produces additional unit directions.

Divisor localization gives

\[
 0\longrightarrow\mathcal O(U)^*/k^*
 \longrightarrow\mathbb Z[R]\oplus\mathbb Z[E]
 \xrightarrow{M_{p,q}}\operatorname{Pic}(X)
 \longrightarrow\operatorname{Pic}(U)
 \longrightarrow0,                                  \tag{11}
\]

where

\[
 M_{p,q}=\begin{pmatrix}q&1\\p&1\end{pmatrix}
 =W_{p,q}^{\mathsf T}.                               \tag{12}
\]

Its Smith normal form yields the following complete calculation.

### Theorem 3.1 (relative-scaling/boundary-lattice theorem)

If `p!=q`, then

\[
 \mathcal O(U)^*=k^*,
 \qquad
 \operatorname{Pic}(U)\simeq\mathbb Z/|q-p|\mathbb Z. \tag{13}
\]

If `p=q`, then

\[
 \mathcal O(U)^*/k^*\simeq\mathbb Z,
 \qquad
 \operatorname{Pic}(U)\simeq\mathbb Z.              \tag{14}
\]

The unit lattice in (14) is generated by the divisor relation
`[R]-p[E]=0`; the corresponding unit is `r/m^p`.

For `d=|q-p|>0`, let

\[
 Y_{p,q}=\{(A,B):m=1,\ \operatorname{Res}(A,B)=1\}.  \tag{15}
\]

Projectivization is the canonical torsor

\[
 Y_{p,q}\longrightarrow U                             \tag{16}
\]

under `mu_d` from (7).  The standard character of this torsor gives, up to
the convention for signs, the restricted tautological line bundle
`O(1,0)|_U`.  Under the explicit identification

\[
 \operatorname{Pic}(U)\longrightarrow\mathbb Z/d\mathbb Z,
 \qquad (x,y)\longmapsto x-y,                         \tag{17}
\]

this line bundle maps to `1`.  Thus the normalization torsor generates the
Picard torsion: the two phenomena are canonically related, not merely of the
same numerical order.  Over an algebraically closed characteristic-zero
field this is also the corresponding statement in the Kummer sequence for
`H^1(U,mu_d)`.

Concretely, the pullback of `O(-1,0)|_U` to `Y_(p,q)` is trivialized by the
tautological representative `A`.  Under the residual action it transforms as
`A -> zeta*A`.  Hence the associated line bundle for the standard character
is `O(-1,0)|_U`, up to the usual left-versus-right action convention.  Its
class maps to `-1` in (17), and therefore generates the cyclic Picard group.

## 4. The canonical bundle always vanishes

The ambient canonical class is

\[
 K_X=(-p-1,-q-1).
\]

There is an integral boundary identity

\[
 \boxed{K_X=[R]-(p+q+1)[E].}                         \tag{18}
\]

Indeed, the right side is

\[
 (q,p)-(p+q+1)(1,1)=(-p-1,-q-1).
\]

Both boundary line bundles become trivial after restriction to `U`.
Therefore

\[
 \boxed{K_U=0}                                       \tag{19}
\]

for every two-factor complement, including the equal-degree case and cases
with nontrivial Picard torsion.  Thus the canonical class never distinguishes
these natural sources from affine space.  For consecutive degrees the same
trivialization follows from viewing (15) as a smooth affine complete
intersection, but (18) explains it uniformly on the projective complement.

## 5. Marked points and the quadratic-cubic example

For the marked-point construction, `(p,q)=(1,n-1)`, so

\[
 q-p=n-2.
\]

The determinant, normalization ambiguity, and Picard torsion in the
marked-point dimension barrier are therefore the same invariant.  The sole
unimodular dimension is `n=3`.

For `(p,q)=(2,3)`, the determinant is one.  The normalized affine slice is
literally the projective complement, its units are constant, and its Picard
and canonical classes vanish.  Nevertheless

\[
 [U_{2,3}]=\mathbb L^5-\mathbb L^3,
\]

so it is not affine five-space.  Unimodularity is a first filter, not a
sufficient affine-space criterion.

## 6. The full multi-factor boundary is the correct filter

Let `s>=3`, let `F_i` have positive degree `d_i`, and consider the ordered
multiplication map

\[
 \prod_{i=1}^s\mathbb P^{d_i}
 \longrightarrow\mathbb P^{d_1+\cdots+d_s}.          \tag{20}
\]

Let `E` be the pullback of a target hyperplane.  To obtain the etale
factorization locus one must also remove every pairwise resultant divisor

\[
 R_{ij}=\{\operatorname{Res}(F_i,F_j)=0\}.
\]

These are distinct prime divisors.  In the standard basis of the ambient
Picard group `Z^s`, their classes are

\[
 [E]=(1,\ldots,1),
 \qquad
 [R_{ij}]=d_j e_i+d_i e_j.                            \tag{21}
\]

### Theorem 6.1 (multi-factor unit obstruction)

Let `h` be the number of prime components of `E`.  For the natural
multi-factor complement `U_s`,

\[
 \operatorname{rank}\bigl(\mathcal O(U_s)^*/k^*\bigr)
 =\binom{s}{2}+h-s.                                  \tag{22}
\]

In particular,

\[
 \operatorname{rank}\bigl(\mathcal O(U_s)^*/k^*\bigr)
 \ge \frac{(s-1)(s-2)}2>0,                           \tag{23}
\]

and hence

\[
 \boxed{U_s\not\simeq\mathbb A^{d_1+\cdots+d_s}}
 \qquad(s\ge3).                                     \tag{24}
\]

### Proof

There are `binom(s,2)+h` prime boundary components.  The resultant classes
alone span `Q^s`: for three indices, the three pairwise classes are linearly
independent over `Q`, and for every additional index `j`, the class `R_(1j)`
introduces a nonzero `j`-th coordinate.  Hence the complete boundary-class
map has rank `s`.  Its kernel, which is the unit lattice by divisor
localization, has the rank in (22).  Since affine space has no nonconstant
units, (24) follows.  \(\square\)

For three factors of degrees `a,b,c` and irreducible `E`, the unique unit
direction is already visible in the class relation

\[
 \begin{aligned}
 2abc[E]={}&c(a+b-c)[R_{12}]\\
            &+b(a+c-b)[R_{13}]
             +a(b+c-a)[R_{23}].                     \tag{25}
 \end{aligned}
\]

Negative coefficients simply place the corresponding resultant in the
denominator.  Formula (25) gives an explicit scaling-invariant unit as the
associated ratio of powers of the defining equations.

This shows why a selected square matrix of normalization weights is
insufficient in the multi-factor setting.  Such a minor may be unimodular,
but units and Picard groups are controlled by the matrix of **all prime
boundary components**.  The latter has an unavoidable kernel for natural
ordered factorizations with at least three factors.

## 7. General matrix formulation

More generally, let a scaling torus act on affine representatives and choose
nonvanishing semi-invariants with integer weight matrix `W`.  Two related
matrices must be distinguished:

1. `W tensor k` is the logarithmic differential on the scaling torus;
2. the kernel of the torus homomorphism defined by `W` is the normalization
   ambiguity;
3. after factoring all boundary equations, the matrix `B` of the classes of
   **all prime boundary components** has kernel equal to the unit lattice;
4. the cokernel of `B` is the first Picard obstruction.

When the chosen semi-invariant divisors are prime and constitute the whole
boundary, `B` is the transpose of `W`, as in the two-factor theorem.  In
general it need not be square and need not be a transpose: factoring one
semi-invariant can create several columns.  The multi-factor obstruction is
exactly this distinction.

The Smith normal form, rather than the determinant alone, is the complete
finite invariant.  Full rank gives an isogeny; it gives etaleness only when
the invariant factors are invertible in the ground field.  Unimodularity
gives unique normalization and removes the square lattice obstruction.  A
rank defect leaves a positive-dimensional scaling kernel and a free
unit/Picard contribution.

For future factorization constructions the correct order of tests is
therefore:

1. form the relative-scaling weight matrix and test its rank over `k`;
2. compute its Smith normal form to determine finite normalization ambiguity;
3. factor the entire projective boundary into prime divisors;
4. compute the full boundary-class matrix, not merely a convenient square
   minor;
5. only after these lattice tests pass, use motives, point counts, topology,
   or finer affine geometry.

## 8. What remains after the lattice theorem

For the natural affine-space question, the lattice stage is now complete:

- equal degrees fail through a free unit and Picard class;
- unequal nonconsecutive degrees fail through Picard torsion;
- natural ordered factorizations with at least three factors fail through
  nonconstant units;
- only two-factor consecutive degrees survive all first lattice tests.

The remaining research is therefore sharply localized rather than open
ended.  For `(1,2)`, the tangent-nonosculating hyperplane gives the known
affine three-space source.  For `(2,3)`, the natural tangent hyperplane fails
by its motivic class, but other binary-quintic hyperplanes have not been
classified.  For `(p,p+1)` with `p>=2`, the next questions are:

1. determine which hyperplane pullbacks are prime and stratify their contact
   types, including moduli once the rational-normal-curve contact divisor has
   more than three support points;
2. compute Grothendieck classes or finite-field counts of the surviving
   complements before attempting explicit affine coordinates;
3. compute topology only for candidates not already separated by those
   realizations;
4. in positive characteristic, analyze the non-etale `mu_d` normalization
   kernel when the characteristic divides `d`.

Thus the determinant topic itself has reached a natural endpoint.  What
remains is the finer geometry of the consecutive two-factor row, not another
variant of the same scaling calculation.
