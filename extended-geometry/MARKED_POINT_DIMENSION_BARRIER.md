# The marked-point dimension barrier

> **Status and scope.**  This note proves an obstruction for the complete
> marked-point/hyperplane-complement construction on `P^1`.  It does not prove
> the two-dimensional Jacobian conjecture and does not classify arbitrary
> Keller maps or arbitrary normalization boundaries.

Work over an algebraically closed field `k` of characteristic zero.  For
`n>=2`, consider the finite marking morphism

\[
 \pi_n:\mathbb P^1\times\operatorname{Sym}^{n-1}(\mathbb P^1)
 \longrightarrow\operatorname{Sym}^n(\mathbb P^1),\qquad
 (x,Q)\longmapsto x+Q.
\]

Using `Sym^d(P^1)=P^d`, write

\[
 M_n=\mathbb P^1\times\mathbb P^{n-1},\qquad
 \operatorname{Sym}^n(\mathbb P^1)=\mathbb P^n.
\]

Let `R_n` be the ramification divisor, equivalently

\[
 R_n=\{(x,Q):Q\text{ contains }x\},
\]

and let `H subset P^n` be a hyperplane.  Put

\[
 E_H=\pi_n^{-1}(H),\qquad
 U_n(H)=M_n\setminus(R_n\cup E_H).
\]

The target complement is `P^n minus H = A^n`, and

\[
 \pi_n|_{U_n(H)}:U_n(H)\longrightarrow\mathbb A^n
\]

is etale and generically `n`-to-one.  The question is when the source open can
itself be affine `n`-space.

## Theorem

### Marked-point dimension barrier

If

\[
 U_n(H)\simeq\mathbb A^n,
\]

then `n=3` and `E_H` is irreducible.

More precisely:

1. if `E_H` is reducible, then `U_n(H)` has a nonconstant unit;
2. if `E_H` is irreducible and `n=2`, then
   \[
    \mathcal O(U_n(H))^*/k^*\simeq\mathbb Z,
    \qquad \operatorname{Pic}(U_n(H))\simeq\mathbb Z;
   \]
3. if `E_H` is irreducible and `n>=3`, then
   \[
    \mathcal O(U_n(H))^*=k^*,
    \qquad
    \operatorname{Pic}(U_n(H))\simeq\mathbb Z/(n-2)\mathbb Z.
   \]

Thus the boundary lattice permits affine space only in dimension three.

## Proof

The Picard group of the ambient product is

\[
 \operatorname{Pic}(M_n)
 =\mathbb Z[\mathcal O(1,0)]\oplus
  \mathbb Z[\mathcal O(0,1)].                         \tag{1}
\]

The two boundary classes are immediate from the universal multiplication of
binary forms.

First, `E_H` is a hyperplane pullback.  The multiplication map is bilinear in
the marked linear factor and the residual degree-`n-1` form, so

\[
 [E_H]=(1,1).                                          \tag{2}
\]

Second, `R_n` is the resultant divisor between a linear form and a
 degree-`n-1` form.  For fixed `Q`, the condition `Q(x)=0` has degree `n-1` in
`x`; for fixed `x`, it is linear in the coefficients of `Q`.  Hence

\[
 [R_n]=(n-1,1).                                        \tag{3}
\]

The incidence divisor `R_n` is irreducible.

Write

\[
 E_H=E_1\cup\cdots\cup E_s
\]

for its irreducible components.  Divisor localization gives the exact
sequence

\[
 k^*\longrightarrow\mathcal O(U_n(H))^*
 \xrightarrow{\operatorname{div}}
 \mathbb Z[R_n]\oplus\bigoplus_{i=1}^s\mathbb Z[E_i]
 \longrightarrow\operatorname{Pic}(M_n)
 \longrightarrow\operatorname{Pic}(U_n(H))\longrightarrow0.       \tag{4}
\]

If `s>=2`, the middle free group has rank at least three while (1) has rank
two.  Its map to `Pic(M_n)` therefore has a nonzero kernel.  Exactness of (4)
produces a nonconstant unit on `U_n(H)`.  This proves the first assertion.

Now suppose `E_H` is irreducible.  In the bases used above, the boundary map
in (4) is represented by

\[
 A_n=
 \begin{pmatrix}
 n-1&1\\
 1&1
 \end{pmatrix},
 \qquad \det A_n=n-2.                                  \tag{5}
\]

For `n=2`, the kernel is generated by `[R_2]-[E_H]`, while the cokernel is
infinite cyclic.  This gives assertion 2.

For `n>=3`, the matrix is injective and its Smith normal form has nontrivial
invariant factor `n-2`.  Therefore

\[
 \ker A_n=0,
 \qquad
 \operatorname{coker}A_n\simeq\mathbb Z/(n-2)\mathbb Z,
\]

which is assertion 3.  Since affine space has only constant units and trivial
Picard group, (4)--(5) leave only `n=3`.  In that case `det A_3=1`, so the two
boundary classes form a basis of `Pic(M_3)`.  This proves the theorem.

## The complete two-dimensional obstruction

For `n=2`, the source compactification is `P^1 x P^1`, the marked-point cover
is

\[
 \pi_2:\mathbb P^1\times\mathbb P^1
 \longrightarrow\operatorname{Sym}^2(\mathbb P^1)\simeq\mathbb P^2,
\]

and the ramification divisor is the diagonal `Delta`.  Both `Delta` and an
irreducible hyperplane pullback have class `(1,1)`.  Their defining sections
therefore have a ratio which is a nonconstant invertible regular function on
the complement.

For the tangent line at the double point `2p`, the pullback is reducible:

\[
 \pi_2^{-1}(H_p)
 =\bigl(\{p\}\times\mathbb P^1\bigr)
  \cup
  \bigl(\mathbb P^1\times\{p\}\bigr).
\]

Taking `p=infinity` gives the explicit source

\[
 U_2(H_p)
 \simeq(\mathbb A^1\times\mathbb A^1)\setminus\Delta
 =\operatorname{Spec}k[s,t,(s-t)^{-1}],                \tag{6}
\]

where `s-t` is a nonconstant unit.  Thus no choice of target line turns the
unramified degree-two marking cover into a Keller map `A^2 -> A^2`.

There is also a geometric formulation.  A tangent line to the Veronese conic
of double points is automatically fully osculating: its contact multiplicity
two consumes the whole intersection.  There is no residual point available
to trivialize the source complement.

## Why dimension three escapes

For `n=3`, the two boundary classes are

\[
 [R_3]=(2,1),\qquad [E_H]=(1,1),
\]

and form a unimodular basis of `Pic(P^1 x P^2)`.  The unit and Picard
obstructions both disappear.

The same distinction appears in the normalized inverse fiber.  A generic
quadratic discriminant point is only

\[
 2p,
\]

so marking its repeated point accounts for the entire degree-two fiber.  A
generic cubic discriminant point is

\[
 2p+q.
\]

The two markings supported at `p` form the length-two boundary contribution,
while marking the residual simple point `q` leaves an affine companion sheet.
This is the normalization-at-infinity reason that the cubic construction can
exist while its quadratic analogue cannot.

## Classification inside the full marked-point family

The complete active cubic contact proof in the
[foundational incidence construction](../verified/FOUNDATIONAL_INCIDENCE_CONSTRUCTION.md)
classifies the three hyperplane contact types for `n=3`.  Only contact type
`(2,1)`, tangent but nonosculating to the twisted cubic, gives `A^3`; the
other two types have different motivic classes and finite-field point counts.

Here the orbit statement gives literal isomorphisms of the normalized affine
sources, not only equality of contact partitions.  If `h,h'` both have
divisor `2p+q` on the twisted cubic, choose `g in PGL_2` carrying `(p,q)` to
the corresponding ordered pair for `h'`.  On binary factors, `g` commutes
with multiplication.  For nonzero constants `delta,eta` one has

\[
 \operatorname{Res}(gL,gQ)=\delta\operatorname{Res}(L,Q),
 \qquad h'(g(LQ))=\eta h(LQ).
\]

After applying `g`, rescale the two factors by

\[
 \lambda={\eta\over\delta},\qquad
 \mu={\delta\over\eta^2}.
\]

Then `lambda*mu*eta=1` and `lambda^2*mu*delta=1`, so both normalized
equations `h'(LQ)=1` and `Res(L,Q)=1` are restored.  This gives an explicit
linear isomorphism `X_h -> X_(h')`.  Thus every tangent-nonosculating
hyperplane gives the same `A^3` source as the representative in the
[normalized factorization model](../verified/NORMALIZED_FACTORIZATION_MODEL.md).

The determinant calculation here is the `(p,q)=(1,n-1)` specialization of
the [relative-scaling/boundary-lattice theorem](RELATIVE_SCALING_BOUNDARY_LATTICE.md),
which also identifies the normalization torsor and proves the corresponding
multi-factor unit obstruction.

The exact cubic fiber stratification gives the stronger motivic table

\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
\text{contact type}&[U_3(H)]&\#U_3(H)(\mathbb F_q)\\ \hline
(1,1,1)&L^3-L&q^3-q\\
(2,1)&L^3&q^3\\
(3)&L^3-L^2&q^3-q^2.
\end{array}
\]

Hence the transverse and osculating orbits are excluded from affine
three-space without relying only on Euler characteristic.

Combining that calculation with the theorem above gives:

### Corollary

Across all `n>=2` and all hyperplanes `H`,

\[
 U_n(H)\simeq\mathbb A^n
\]

if and only if `n=3` and `H` has tangent nonosculating contact type `(2,1)`.

This identifies the known cubic marked-point construction as the unique
affine-space member of the universal marked-point/hyperplane-complement
family.

## Scope boundary

The theorem excludes the exact construction pattern

\[
 \text{mark one point of a divisor on }\mathbb P^1,
 \quad
 \text{remove ramification and one pulled-back hyperplane}.
\]

It does **not** show that every hypothetical plane Keller map must admit such
a presentation.  In particular, it does not rule out a more complicated
finite normalization with several boundary primes and affine companion
sheets above every nonproperness component.  The two-dimensional Jacobian
conjecture remains a separate problem.

In short: JC(3) exposes a concrete marked-point mechanism, and this theorem
excludes that exact mechanism in dimension two.  It does not describe what
all counterexamples must look like and does not exclude arbitrary JC(2)
counterexamples.

The independently audited [Newton-polygon degree frontier](../plane-jc/DEGREE_FRONTIER_125.md)
has complementary scope:

| This obstruction | Newton degree-frontier work |

| Degree-independent | Gives a numerical lower bound for the larger coordinate degree |

Consequently, any hypothetical JC(2) counterexample must avoid the
marked-point/hyperplane-complement mechanism and must lie beyond the current
degree frontier.  The two statements do not together prove JC(2).  The
qualified Newton-to-boundary dictionary and the presently unsuccessful search
