# Primitive merger hypergraphs and omitted-component intersection algebras

Work over a field `k` of characteristic zero.  This note gives the general
form of the Boolean algebra seen in the degree-twelve double intersection and
the degree-eighteen ordered triple intersection.  It also identifies the
first failure.

There are two logically different assertions:

1. formal Hensel separation determines the intersection with the **strong**
   equation that all omitted polynomials are equal; and
2. equality of normalized seeds only says that their omitted polynomials
   differ by affine polynomials.

The first assertion is combinatorial and holds uniformly.  Passing from it
to the actual normalized-seed intersection requires a geometric upper bound.
The quadratic-sandwich criterion below supplies exactly that bound.  Thus
“independent” has a precise partition-hypergraph meaning, but the claim that
the projected intersection has no extra infinitesimal structure is not a
purely combinatorial statement.

## 1. The allocation partition hypergraph

Fix an exact ordered collision polynomial

\[
 M_0=\prod_{\rho\in R}(W-r_\rho)^{m_\rho},
 \qquad r_\rho\ne r_\sigma\quad(\rho\ne\sigma).
\]

A normalization sheet `s` over this point assigns to every collision block
an allocation

\[
 \alpha_s(\rho)=(i_{s\rho},j_{s\rho}),\qquad
 2i_{s\rho}+3j_{s\rho}=m_\rho.
\]

For a finite ordered set `Sigma` of sheets, the **allocation partition
hypergraph** has vertex set `Sigma`.  The hyperedge indexed by `rho` is the
partition of `Sigma` into the fibers of

\[
 s\longmapsto\alpha_s(\rho).
\]

The edge is active when this map is nonconstant.  It is a **separated
primitive merger edge** when

\[
 m_\rho=6,
 \qquad \alpha_s(\rho)\in\{(3,0),(0,2)\}.
\]

The family is **Hensel-independent** when every active edge is a separated
primitive merger edge.  Put

\[
 t=t(\Sigma)=\#\{\rho:\rho\text{ is active}\}.       \tag{1}
\]

The word independent in this definition means that the operations occur in
distinct completed local factors.  It does **not** mean that allocation
vectors, cut vectors, or merger paths are linearly independent.

For primitive edges write `x_s(rho)=1` for `(3,0)` and `0` for `(0,2)`.
Within a fixed component all binary words have the same weight.  Its
**branch-merger graph** has the chosen sheets as vertices and joins two
sheets when their words differ in exactly two positions.  Such an edge is
the elementary compensated swap

\[
 e_\rho-e_\sigma.
\]

The ordinary cycle space

\[
 Z_1(G;k)=\ker\bigl(k^{E(G)}\longrightarrow k^{V(G)}\bigr) \tag{2}
\]

records relations among ordered merger paths.  If `G` has `c` connected
components, then

\[
 \dim Z_1(G;k)=|E(G)|-|V(G)|+c.                    \tag{3}
\]

It will not enter the exponent in the intersection length.

## 2. The primitive Hensel-product theorem

Let `D^=_Sigma` be the completed common-factorization correspondence of the
sheets in `Sigma`, with the strong equations

\[
 M_s=M_{s'}\qquad(s,s'\in\Sigma).
\]

Complete at the geometric generic point of the exact ordered collision
stratum and slice transverse to its reduced support.

### Theorem 2.1

If the allocation partition hypergraph is Hensel-independent, then

\[
 \boxed{
 D^{=,\mathrm{tr}}_\Sigma\simeq
 B_t:=k[\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_t]/
       (\epsilon_1^2,\ldots,\epsilon_t^2).}          \tag{4}
\]

Consequently

\[
 \operatorname{length}B_t=2^t,qquad
 \operatorname{edim}B_t=t,qquad
 \operatorname{Hilb}B_t=(1+z)^t.                   \tag{5}
\]

Its socle is one-dimensional and is generated by the nonzero class

\[
 \epsilon_1\cdots\epsilon_t.                       \tag{6}
\]

Before taking the transverse slice, the tangent excess over the exact
collision stratum is `t`.

### Proof

Formal Hensel factorization of a monic deformation of `M_0` is unique because
the factors centered at the distinct `r_rho` are pairwise coprime.  Hence
the strong equality of global polynomials is equivalent to equality of the
monic cluster polynomials separately at every `rho`.

At an inactive edge all sheets use the same allocation.  The corresponding
factorization maps are formally injective after fixing the monic cluster
polynomial, so this part of the correspondence is a smooth diagonal factor.

At an active primitive edge there are only two allocation types.  Repeated
occurrences of one type add diagonal copies and no new equation.  After
translation of the collision root, the one nontrivial local correspondence
is therefore

\[
 Q^2=T^3,qquad \deg Q=3,\quad\deg T=2.
\]

Depress the cubic and write

\[
 Q=Z^3+uZ+v,qquad T=Z^2+aZ+b.
\]

Coefficient comparison gives the exact ideal

\[
 (a,v,2u-3b,b^2).                                  \tag{7}
\]

After the smooth root-position coordinate is removed, (7) is one copy of
`k[epsilon]/(epsilon^2)`.  Hensel uniqueness makes the completed global
correspondence the completed tensor product of the local correspondences.
The `t` active edges therefore give (4).

On the dense open where the incidence equation `Phi=0` has nonzero
derivative in a support coordinate, the formal implicit-function theorem
removes one smooth parameter and no transverse parameter.  Equations
(5)--(6) now follow from the squarefree monomial basis

\[
 \epsilon_I=\prod_{i\in I}\epsilon_i,qquad I\subseteq\{1,\ldots,t\}.
 \quad\square
\]

## 3. Why cycles do not impose nilpotent relations

An elementary merger path changes binary allocation words, so its oriented
edge labels lie in the sum-zero lattice on the active roots.  A cycle gives
a linear dependence among those labels.  The nilpotent `epsilon_rho`,
however, is not an edge label: it is the transverse class of the completed
factorization correspondence at the single Hensel cluster `rho`.

Thus merger paths are controlled by the boundary map of the graph, while
the intersection algebra is controlled by the product decomposition over
collision roots.  These are different linearizations.  In particular,

\[
 \boxed{\text{cycles do not subtract from }t.}       \tag{8}
\]

For `C_(3,4)` over `(6,6,6)`, the three sheets are the singleton subsets of
three roots.  Their merger graph is the triangle `K_3`, with cycle-space
dimension one.  All three root hyperedges are active, so Theorem 2.1 gives
`t=3`, length eight, and the nonzero cubic class in (6).  For one pair of
sheets only two root edges are active, giving length four.

The same distinction persists in the first larger face.  The four singleton
allocation sheets over `(6,6,6,6)` have merger graph `K_4`, whose cycle rank
is three, while their strong transverse algebra has four generators and
length sixteen.

## 4. From strong equality to normalized-seed equality

Let `D_Sigma` be the actual completed common intersection of the normalized
seed images.  Equality of normalized seeds permits

\[
 M_s-M_{s'}\in\langle1,W\rangle,                   \tag{9}
\]

so the strong correspondence is a closed subscheme:

\[
 D^=_\Sigma\hookrightarrow D_\Sigma.              \tag{10}
\]

The following criterion turns the Boolean lower bound into an equality.

### Theorem 4.1 (quadratic sandwich)

Assume the allocation hypergraph is Hensel-independent with `t` active
edges.  Slice `D_Sigma` transverse to its reduced support and let `A` be its
complete local Artin ring.  Suppose:

1. `A` has embedding dimension `t`; and
2. in a minimal presentation `A=k[[x_1,...,x_t]]/I`, the initial ideal of
   `I` contains a homogeneous regular sequence of `t` quadrics.

Then

\[
 \boxed{A\simeq B_t.}                              \tag{11}
\]

### Proof

The closed immersion (10) gives a surjection `A -> B_t`, so
`length(A)>=2^t`.  The regular sequence of `t` quadrics has quotient length
`2^t`.  Since the associated graded ring of `A` is a quotient of that
quadratic complete intersection,

\[
 \operatorname{length}A=\operatorname{length}\operatorname{gr}A\le2^t.
\]

The surjection has equal finite lengths and is therefore an isomorphism.
`square`

Condition 2 may equivalently be checked by finding `t` quadratic obstruction
forms with zero-dimensional common zero and colength `2^t`.  This is exactly
the upper-bound calculation in the degree-twelve and degree-eighteen notes.
The theorem therefore recovers both established projected intersections.

The criterion also states the proof boundary precisely.  Hensel
independence predicts the lower algebra and expected tangent excess from the
partition hypergraph.  It does not, by itself, prove that projection away
from the affine coefficients creates no additional infinitesimal points.
That assertion is a second-fundamental-form, Rees-strictness, or equivalent
upper-bound problem.

## 5. The first failure: two mergers at one root

The first failure of the Boolean multiplication occurs when two primitive
sixfold clusters coalesce.  At one twelvefold root the local transfer has

\[
 \deg U=6,qquad\deg V=4,qquad U^2=V^3.
\]

Write

\[
 S=Z^2+pZ+q,qquad V=S^2+XZ+Y.
\]

Triangular elimination of the six nonleading coefficients of `U` gives

\[
 \boxed{
 Z_2=k[p,q,X,Y]/(X^3,\ 2XY-pX^2,\ Y^2-qX^2).}       \tag{12}
\]

It is free of rank four over `k[p,q]`, with basis

\[
 1,X,Y,X^2.                                         \tag{13}
\]

Over the squarefree locus of `S`, etale Hensel separation turns (12) into a
tensor product of two dual-number blocks.  At the coincident polynomial
`S=Z^2`, however, its transverse fiber is

\[
 \boxed{k[X,Y]/(X^3,XY,Y^2).}                       \tag{14}
\]

Both (14) and `B_2` have length four and Hilbert vector `(1,2,1)`, but they
are not isomorphic.  The socle of (14) is

\[
 kX^2\oplus kY,
\]

whereas the socle of `B_2` is the one-dimensional space
`k epsilon_1 epsilon_2`.  Thus (14) is not Gorenstein and `B_2` is
Gorenstein.

This is the first possible failure: one primitive transfer gives (7), while
two transfers already fit in one multiplicity-twelve cluster.  It shows that
“independent” cannot mean only that there are two tangent directions or that
the expected length is four.  It must include separation into distinct
Hensel factors.

## 6. Resulting general prediction

For a finite family of sheets define the block amplitude at `rho` from the
allocation differences

\[
 i_{s\rho}-i_{s'\rho}=3k_\rho(s,s'),\qquad
 j_{s\rho}-j_{s'\rho}=-2k_\rho(s,s').              \tag{15}
\]

The uniform local object at a nonprimitive active edge is a higher transfer
block `Z_h`, not `h` labeled dual numbers, where `h` is the effective
transfer amplitude after common factors are cancelled.  Hensel separation
still predicts a completed tensor product over the **distinct collision
roots**:

\[
 D^{=,\mathrm{tr}}_\Sigma
 \simeq\widehat\bigotimes_{\rho\ \mathrm{active}}Z_{\rho,\Sigma}.
                                                               \tag{16}
\]

Theorem 2.1 is the completely classified case in which every factor in (16)
is `Z_1`.  Formula (12) classifies the first nonprimitive two-sheet factor
`Z_2` and disproves a universal Boolean identification on collision strata.

Accordingly, the next genuinely new projected case is not another random
degree census.  It is the degree-twenty-four ordered quadruple over
`(6,6,6,6)`: Theorem 2.1 predicts the strong algebra `B_4`, and Theorem 4.1
reduces the actual normalized-seed statement to a four-variable quadratic
colength-sixteen obstruction calculation.  In parallel, a uniform theory of
the higher blocks in (16) must retain their multiplication, not merely their
observed length `2^h`.
