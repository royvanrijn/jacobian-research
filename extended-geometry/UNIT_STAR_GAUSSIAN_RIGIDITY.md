# Prime rigidity for unit-star Gaussian supports

## 1. The unit-star family

Let \(S\subset\mathbb Z_{>0}\) be finite and consider
\[
P=T B(U)+C(U)+\sum_{d\in S}T^{-d}U^dA_d(U).       \tag{1.1}
\]
For each negative-weight leaf define
\[
X_d=U^dA_dB^d,\qquad \ell_d=d+1,                 \tag{1.2}
\]
and put \(X_0=C,\ \ell_0=1\).

The zero-weight relation monoid is free on the relations
\(d(1)+(-d)=0\).  If the negative component of weight \(-d\) is used
\(q_d\) times, balance uniquely forces \(TB\) to be used
\(\sum_d d q_d\) times.  Hence
\[
\boxed{
H_m:=\operatorname{CT}_T(P^m)=
\sum_{\substack{k,q_d\geq0\\k+\sum_d(d+1)q_d=m}}
\frac{m!}{k!\,(\sum_d d q_d)!\,\prod_dq_d!}
C^k\prod_dX_d^{q_d}.
}                                                 \tag{1.3}
\]
The Gaussian moments are \(\mathbb E(P^m)=\mathcal L(H_m)\), with
\(\mathcal L(U^j)=j!\).

## 2. The smallest genuine star

For
\[
P=TB_1+C+T^{-1}UA_1+T^{-2}U^2A_2,               \tag{2.1}
\]
put
\[
X=UA_1B_1,\qquad Y=U^2A_2B_1^2.                 \tag{2.2}
\]
Then
\[
H_m(C,X,Y)=
\sum_{2r+3q\leq m}
\frac{m!}{(r+2q)!\,r!\,q!\,(m-2r-3q)!}
C^{m-2r-3q}X^rY^q.                              \tag{2.3}
\]
In particular,
\[
\begin{aligned}
H_1&=C,\\
H_2&=C^2+2X,\\
H_3&=C^3+6CX+3Y,\\
H_4&=C^4+12C^2X+6X^2+12CY.                      \tag{2.4}
\end{aligned}
\]
For every prime \(p>3\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
H_p&\equiv C^p,\\
H_{2p}&\equiv C^{2p}+2X^p,\\
H_{3p}&\equiv C^{3p}+6C^pX^p+3Y^p
\pmod p.                                        \tag{2.5}
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus \(C,X,Y\) first occur as prime endpoints at indices \(p,2p,3p\).

If
\[
a=\operatorname{ord}_U C,\quad
x=\operatorname{ord}_U X,\quad
y=\operatorname{ord}_U Y,                        \tag{2.6}
\]
the normalized orders \(a,x/2,y/3\) give three exhaustive cases:

1. \(H_p\) isolates \(C^p\) if \(a\leq x/2\) and \(a\leq y/3\);
2. \(H_{2p}\) isolates \(2X^p\) if \(x/2<a\) and \(x/2\leq y/3\);
3. \(H_{3p}\) isolates \(3Y^p\) if \(y/3<a\) and \(y/3<x/2\).

Weak inequalities belong to the shorter circuit, so every tie is covered.

## 3. Prime dilation

> **Prime-dilation lemma.** Let \(p\) be prime and \(1\leq n<p\).  Over
> every \(\mathbb F_p\)-algebra,
> \[
> H_{np}=H_n^p.                                  \tag{3.1}
> \]

**Proof.** In characteristic \(p\), \(P^{np}=(P^n)^p\).  Every Laurent
exponent on the right is multiplied by \(p\), so constant-term extraction
commutes with Frobenius. \(\square\)

## 4. Unit-star rigidity

> **Unit-star rigidity theorem.** If (1.1) satisfies
> \[
> \mathbb E(P^m)=0\qquad(m\geq1),
> \]
> then
> \[
> C=0,\qquad B=0\quad\text{or}\quad
> A_d=0\ \text{for every }d\in S.                \tag{4.1}
> \]
> Hence \(P\) has one-sided rotational support and satisfies
> \(\operatorname{GMC}(2)\).

**Proof.** Suppose some invariant \(X_i\), including \(X_0\), is nonzero.
Put
\[
\nu_i=\operatorname{ord}_U X_i,\qquad
\rho_i=\frac{\nu_i}{\ell_i}.                     \tag{4.2}
\]
Choose \(i\) with minimum \(\rho_i\), breaking ties by minimum \(\ell_i\).
The lengths \(1,d+1\) are distinct, so \(i\) is unique.  Write
\(\ell=\ell_i,\nu=\nu_i\), and let \(c_i\) be the lowest coefficient of
\(X_i\).

Every monomial in \(H_{\ell p}\) has total circuit length \(\ell p\), so
its \(U\)-order is at least
\[
\rho_i\ell p=\nu p.                              \tag{4.3}
\]
In \(H_\ell\), the monomial \(X_i\) has coefficient \(1\) for \(i=0\), and
coefficient
\[
\frac{(d+1)!}{d!}=d+1=\ell                       \tag{4.4}
\]
for \(i=d\).  Every other monomial has order strictly greater than \(\nu\):
equality in (4.3) would use only minimum-slope invariants, all of whose
lengths are at least \(\ell\), and the unique invariant of length \(\ell\)
is \(X_i\).

Use the finite-type coefficient ring generated by all coefficients, with
\(c_i^{-1}\) adjoined.  For all but finitely many primes it has a reduction
in which \(c_i\ne0\).  Choose one with \(p>\ell\).  Cancel \((\nu p)!\)
from \(\mathcal L(H_{\ell p})=0\) using (4.3), then reduce modulo \(p\).
By (3.1), \(H_{\ell p}=H_\ell^p\).  The unique lowest term leaves
\[
\ell c_i^p
\]
(with \(\ell=1\) for \(C\)).  Higher Frobenius terms have degrees \(jp\)
with \(j>\nu\), so their factorial quotients cross \((\nu+1)p\).  The
displayed element is nonzero because \(p>\ell\) and \(c_i\) is a unit, a
contradiction.

Thus every \(X_i\) is zero.  In particular \(C=0\) and
\(U^dA_dB^d=0\) for every \(d\).  Since \(\mathbb C[U]\) is a domain,
either \(B=0\), or every \(A_d=0\).  One-sided weight gives GMC(2).
\(\square\)

This is valuation-minimal leaf removal, but it closes the entire unit star
at once: assuming any invariant remains simply selects another minimum.

## 5. Why the graph-only forest theorem needs more data

The proof uses the freeness of the zero-weight relation monoid, not merely
the abstract circuit-incidence tree.  With a unique positive weight
\(h>1\), mixed integral relations appear.

For weights \(\{5,-2,-3\}\), the pairwise leaf circuits have lengths \(7\)
and \(8\), but
\[
5-2-3=0                                          \tag{5.1}
\]
gives a mixed relation of length \(3\).  For
\[
P=T^5B+T^{-2}U^2A_2+T^{-3}U^3A_3,
\]
it produces
\[
J=U^5BA_2A_3,                                    \tag{5.2}
\]
which is not a product of the pair invariants in the polynomial invariant
semigroup.

Therefore â€śa tree has a prime-isolated leafâ€ť does not follow from the
circuit-incidence graph alone.  The unit-star theorem proves every support
\(\{0,1,-d_1,\ldots,-d_q\}\) and its reflection, but literal leaf removal
does not directly extend to arbitrary weights.

The [lower-face theorem](TWO_REAL_GMC_LOWER_FACE_THEOREM.md) bypasses this
semigroup obstruction.  It packages all relations on the exposed
radial-order Newton face into one Laurent polynomial, applies the
Duistermaat--van der Kallen constant-term theorem there, and then uses prime
dilation to isolate its first nonzero constant term.  That stronger argument
proves arbitrary-star rigidity, the full circuit-forest conjecture, and in
fact all of \(\operatorname{GMC}(2)\).
