# Contact monoids for Hessian--Ritt intersections

## Verdict

The analogy with collision local rings is real, but the first formulation
needs one refinement.

The relation graph determines the reduced Ritt family and, after
synchronization, its power/Dickson normalization.  It does not determine
the integral orders with which common-factor, overlap, and projection
ideals meet that normalization.  Those orders form a **Ritt contact
monoid**.  The degree-thirty braid shows that this monoid still cannot
determine the unreduced local ring by itself: the three labelled thick
sectors have different transverse complete-intersection algebras, and their
nilpotents are coupled to different powers of the power--Dickson overlap
parameter.

The viable invariant is therefore

\[
 \boxed{
 \text{relation 2-complex}
 +\text{integral contact monoid}
 +\text{contact-graded defect algebra}
 +\text{synchronization complex}.}
 \tag{0.1}
\]

Here the word *contact-graded* is essential.  The defect algebra is an
algebra over the completed monoid ring, not merely an abstract transverse
Artin algebra.  It must remember, for example, that the normalization-defect
module is annihilated by \(z^2\) or \(z^4\).  Likewise the synchronization
object should initially be a two-term complex, rather than only its
degree-zero module, because failure to lift a reduced common factor through
a primary thickening is an obstruction problem.

This gives a sharpened Ritt-contact conjecture and a concrete computational
programme.  It also explains why classical Ritt theory controls the reduced
supports but not the projected nilpotent structures.

## 1. The local setup

Let \(H\) be the exact-degree monic Hessian coefficient chart and let
\[
 X_{\mathcal D}=\bigcap_{\alpha\in\mathcal D} C_\alpha\subset H
 \tag{1.1}
\]
be an intersection of requested Hessian-composition cuts.  Fix a point
\(x\) on a reduced component \(Y\subset (X_{\mathcal D})_{\rm red}\), and
let
\[
 \nu:\widetilde Y\longrightarrow Y
 \tag{1.2}
\]
be the normalized power/Dickson parametrization selected by its relation
graph.  Work after a finite residue-field extension and at a point
\(\widetilde x\) above \(x\).

Write
\[
 B=\widehat{\mathcal O}_{\widetilde Y,\widetilde x}.
 \tag{1.3}
\]
On the tame clean chart \(B\) is a formal power-series ring.  Near the
boundary, distinguish the following ideals on the factor charts.

1. **Ritt-overlap ideals.**  These cut out intersections of normal-form
   families, such as the divisor \(z=0\) where a Dickson block degenerates
   to a power block.
2. **Common-factor ideals.**  These are Fitting or subresultant ideals
   measuring equality and degree jump of reconstructed terminal factors,
   approximate roots, and intermediate fields.
3. **Move-contact ideals.**  These are the residual ideals defining the
   coefficient correspondences attached to edges and labelled sectors of
   commuting or braid 2-cells.
4. **Synchronization ideals.**  These are generated by the lift
   differences
   \[
     \delta_{\alpha\beta}=\lambda_\alpha-\lambda_\beta
   \tag{1.4}
   \]
   and, before equality is known, by the coefficient differences of the
   proposed common terminal factors.

This list is deliberately ideal-theoretic.  A divisor support, a relation
graph, or a real tropical cone forgets the integral multiplicities needed
for a completed-local-ring statement.

## 2. The Ritt contact monoid

Assume first that the pullbacks to \(B\) of all ideals in Section 1 can be
simultaneously monomialized.  Choose primitive boundary parameters
\[
 u_1,\ldots,u_r
 \tag{2.1}
\]
on a toroidal modification of \(\widetilde Y\).  Every relevant generator
then has the form
\[
 \text{unit}\cdot u_1^{a_1}\cdots u_r^{a_r}.
 \tag{2.2}
\]

> **Definition 2.1.**  The unsaturated Ritt contact monoid \(P_{G,x}\) is
> the submonoid of \(\mathbb N^r\), with its maps from the labelled
> boundary and move ideals retained, generated by all exponent vectors in
> (2.2).

Equivalently, \(P_{G,x}\) is the value monoid of the normalized multi-Rees
algebra of the ideals in Section 1.  Retaining the maps from the labelled
generators prevents two distinct Ritt sectors with isomorphic abstract
monoids from being identified.

There is an immediate analogue of the collision-local-ring counterexample.
Pull a family meeting the power--Dickson divisor with primitive parameter
\(z\) back along
\[
 z=Z^m.
 \tag{2.3}
\]
The special polynomial, its decompositions, its relation graph, and its
power/Dickson vertex maps are unchanged.  The contact vector of the overlap
divisor is multiplied by \(m\).  If a defect module was annihilated by
\((z^a)\), its pullback is annihilated by \((Z^{ma})\).  Hence the relation
graph and special-factor data alone cannot determine the completed local
ring.  Primitivity of the universal coefficient chart would exclude this
example, but primitivity must be a hypothesis or a proved reconstruction
lemma.

As in the collision setting, saturation should be applied only after
selecting the component and phase:

\[
 B_{G,x}^{\rm tor}
 =
 k'[[P_{G,x}^{\rm sat}]][[w_1,\ldots,w_s]].
 \tag{2.4}
\]

This ring is the candidate normalization of the reduced Ritt component, up
to smooth factors and finite stabilizers.  It is not yet the unreduced
intersection ring.

## 3. The defect algebra must be relative

Let
\[
 R=\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_{\mathcal D},x},\qquad
 K=\sqrt{0_R},
 \tag{3.1}
\]
after restricting to the chosen primary component.  Thus \(R/K\) is the
completed reduced image and \(K\) is its nilradical.  The elementary
transverse symbol
\[
 \operatorname{gr}_K(R)
 =
 \bigoplus_{n\geq0}K^n/K^{n+1}
 \tag{3.2}
\]
records the nilpotence filtration but is generally insufficient: filtered
algebras with the same associated graded need not be isomorphic.

Let \(J_1,\ldots,J_r\) be the labelled contact ideals from Section 2.  The
appropriate first invariant is the multi-filtered symbol
\[
 \mathfrak D_{G,x}
 =
 \operatorname{gr}_{K,J_1,\ldots,J_r}(R),
 \tag{3.3}
\]
together with its algebra structure over \(k[P_{G,x}]\) and the labelled
augmentation
\[
 \mathfrak D_{G,x}\longrightarrow k[P_{G,x}].
 \tag{3.4}
\]
Equivalently, one can retain the completed multi-Rees algebra rather than
only its special fiber.  This stronger version avoids the general
associated-graded ambiguity.

The distinction is visible in the degree-thirty braid.  On the common
Dickson normalization \(B=\mathbb Q[[t,z]]\), the three thick sectors have

\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c|c}
\text{omitted composite cut}&
\text{transverse algebra}&
\text{internal orders}&
\operatorname{Ann}_B(K)\\
\hline
10&\mathbb Q[u]/(u^5)&(5)&(z^2)\\
15&\mathbb Q[u,v]/(u^2,v^2)&(2,2)&(z^2)\\
6&\mathbb Q[u,v]/(u^4,v^2)&(4,2)&(z^4).
\end{array}
\tag{3.5}
\]

The abstract contact ray generated by \(z\) does not distinguish the first
two rows.  The abstract conormal or Tor ranks do not distinguish the last
two rows.  The relative defect algebra, including its \(z\)-action and
internal filtration, distinguishes all three.

This also shows why a tensor-product ansatz is wrong.  The thickening is not
in general
\[
 B\widehat\otimes_k A_{\rm transverse}.
 \tag{3.6}
\]
Its nilpotents are supported on, and coupled to, a contact divisor.  The
correct operation is a contact-graded extension over \(B\).

## 4. The synchronization complex

For every requested cut choose its canonical lift
\[
 F+\lambda_\alpha W=A_\alpha\circ B_\alpha.
 \tag{4.1}
\]
The degree-zero synchronization module is
\[
 M_{\rm sync}
 =
 \frac{\langle\delta_{\alpha\beta}\rangle R}
      {\text{relations induced by lifted common factors and Ritt moves}}.
 \tag{4.2}
\]

This module detects surviving linear-coefficient discrepancies.  It should
not be the whole invariant.  Constructing a common terminal factor over
\(R_{\rm red}\) and lifting it through \(R\) has an obstruction group.
The natural object is therefore a two-term complex
\[
 \mathcal S_{G,x}
 =
 [\,E_{\rm factor}\longrightarrow E_{\rm lift}\,],
 \tag{4.3}
\]
whose \(H^0\) contains the lift differences and whose \(H^1\) contains the
primary obstruction to lifting the reduced gcd refinement.  A concrete
model can be obtained by linearizing the normalized composition equations
in the coefficients of the proposed common right factor.

The top-jet theorem in
[`COMMON_RIGHT_FACTOR_SYNCHRONIZATION.md`](COMMON_RIGHT_FACTOR_SYNCHRONIZATION.md)
proves that \(H^0(\mathcal S_{G,x})=0\) once equal-degree terminal
refinements exist over the local ring.  It does not force
\(H^1(\mathcal S_{G,x})=0\); that is precisely the remaining primary
liftability problem.

On all six certified degree-thirty braid charts, the ordinary polynomial
and Hessian ideals agree.  Thus the braid nilpotents have
\[
 H^0(\mathcal S_{G,x})=0
 \tag{4.4}
\]
and are carried by \(\mathfrak D_{G,x}\), not by a Hessian projection
defect.  This is an important separation of roles.

## 5. Sharpened conjecture

The evidence supports two levels of statement.

> **Reduced Ritt-contact conjecture.**  After selecting a relation-graph
> power/Dickson component and a contact chamber, its normalized completed
> reduced local ring is, up to smooth factors and a finite stabilizer,
> \[
>   k'[[P_{G,x}^{\rm sat}]].
> \tag{5.1}
> \]
> The relation 2-complex selects the component and its move coherences; the
> integral monoid selects the toric chart.

This is the direct analogue of the saturated contact-monoid theorem.  It
requires simultaneous monomial contact, unless the Ritt space is first
constructed by normalized closure over a canonical toroidal base.

> **Scheme-theoretic Ritt-contact conjecture.**  Suppose:
>
> 1. the reduced component has the relation-graph normal form;
> 2. its common-factor and move ideals have simultaneous monomial contact;
> 3. the completed multi-Rees defect algebra is finite over the resulting
>    toric base and is rigid among filtered deformations with its specified
>    contact action; and
> 4. the synchronization complex is specified, including its attaching map
>    to the defect algebra.
>
> Then the completed primary local ring of the Hessian-projected Ritt
> intersection is determined, up to stable strict-etale equivalence, by
> \[
> (G^{(2)},P_{G,x},\mathfrak D_{G,x},\mathcal S_{G,x}).
> \tag{5.2}
> \]

The rigidity clause is not cosmetic.  Without it, an associated-graded
defect algebra does not in general determine its filtered deformation.
One can instead make the conjecture unconditional but stronger by replacing
\(\mathfrak D_{G,x}\) with the full completed multi-Rees algebra.  That
version is closer to a reconstruction theorem, though less economical as a
classification invariant.

## 6. Existing evidence

### 6.1 Reduced power and Dickson blocks

For a generically reduced synchronized block,
\[
 K=0,\qquad \mathfrak D_{G,x}=k[P_{G,x}],\qquad
 H^0(\mathcal S_{G,x})=0.
 \tag{6.1}
\]
The conjecture reduces to the relation-graph power/Dickson classification.
This is exactly the information supplied set-theoretically by classical
Ritt theory and, for tame multi-collisions, by the multi-collision normal
form.

### 6.2 The degree-eighteen nested power component

The family
\[
 \bigl(A(B(W-s))\bigr)^2
 \tag{6.2}
\]
is one reduced exponential relation-graph block carrying several compatible
Ritt moves.  Its known synchronization makes the synchronization module
zero on the certified chart.  The next useful calculation is not another
minimal-prime computation; it is the multi-Rees valuation matrix of the
four pulled-back cut ideals along the boundary divisors of the
\((s,u,v)\)-space.

### 6.3 The degree-thirty braid

The braid supplies the nonreduced test in (3.5).  It proves:

* the bare relation graph does not determine the scheme structure;
* the unlabelled braid 2-cell does not determine the scheme structure;
* the common normalization does not determine the scheme structure;
* abstract Tor ranks do not determine the labelled sector;
* the relative contact action plus the filtered defect algebra distinguishes
  the three computed sectors; and
* the synchronization object is independent of the braid defect on the
  certified charts.

Thus the braid data fit (5.2) substantially better than a conjecture using
only a relation graph and an abstract nilpotent algebra.

## 7. First decisive computations

The following tests would turn the proposal into a theorem or find its first
failure.

1. **Compute the degree-thirty multi-Rees symbols.**  For each thick sector,
   retain simultaneously the nilradical filtration, the overlap ideal
   \((z)\), and the omitted-cut residual ideal.  Verify that the resulting
   bigraded algebra reconstructs the annihilator powers and the three
   transverse presentations in (3.5).
2. **Run the ramified-contact test.**  Substitute \(z=Z^m\) in the exact
   braid ideals for \(m=2,3\).  Check that the relation graph and reduction
   remain fixed while the labelled contact vectors and annihilator powers
   scale by \(m\).
3. **Test filtered rigidity.**  Compute the negative-weight part of
   \(T^1\) for the three contact-graded complete intersections.  Vanishing
   would justify replacing the full multi-Rees algebra by its symbol;
   nonvanishing would produce the missing extension parameters.
4. **Extract the synchronization complex.**  Linearize the common-right-
   factor reconstruction on the degree-thirty and degree-forty-two primary
   charts.  Compare its \(H^0\) with the known lift-difference reductions
   and its \(H^1\) with failure of refinement lifting at the first
   uncertified normal order.
5. **Compare two charts with the same proposed invariant.**  Opposite
   endpoint charts in every braid sector already agree.  The decisive next
   comparison is between different toroidal resolutions of the same
   common-factor ideal: equality after saturation tests whether the proposed
   contact monoid is intrinsic rather than chart-dependent.

The first two tests are finite Gröbner/Rees calculations using ideals already
constructed by the degree-thirty checker.

### 7.1 Completed first structural audit

The ramified-contact and invariant-minimality parts of items 1--2 now have a
checker.  It uses the sector data proved by the full degree-thirty
Gröbner calculation and verifies:

* all three thick sectors have the same reduced \(S_3\) braid 2-cell and
  abstract primitive rank-one overlap monoid;
* the contact annihilator exponent alone conflates the cut-\(10\) and
  cut-\(15\) sectors;
* conormal rank and ungraded Koszul--Tor data conflate the cut-\(15\) and
  cut-\(6\) sectors;
* contact action together with internal complete-intersection orders and
  nilpotence index separates all three sectors; and
* under \(z=Z^m\), the labelled contact vector and annihilator exponent are
  multiplied by \(m\), while the relation cell, transverse algebra, and
  nilpotence index remain unchanged.

The scaling assertion is formal as well as computational.  The map
\[
 k[[z]]\longrightarrow k[[Z]],\qquad z\longmapsto Z^m
\tag{7.1}
\]
is finite free.  Hence base change is faithfully flat.  For a finitely
presented defect module \(N\),
\[
 \operatorname{Ann}_{k[[Z]]}
 \bigl(N\otimes_{k[[z]]}k[[Z]]\bigr)
 =
 \operatorname{Ann}_{k[[z]]}(N)\,k[[Z]].
\tag{7.2}
\]
Thus \((z^a)\) becomes \((Z^{ma})\).  This proves that the labelled integral
contact map, rather than only the abstract rank-one monoid, is necessary.

What is not yet computed is the full bigraded multiplication table of the
multi-Rees symbol.  That is now the unique immediate algebraic task; the
necessity and minimality checks no longer need further examples.

## 8. Relation to classical Ritt theory

Mueller--Zieve describe how complete decompositions are related by Ritt
moves, von zur Gathen gives usable normal forms for the two basic
collisions, and Ziegler gives tame multi-collision normal forms.  These
results classify decompositions of field-valued polynomials and therefore
govern the reduced generic families:

* [On Ritt's polynomial decomposition theorems](https://arxiv.org/abs/0807.3578);
* [Normal form for Ritt's Second Theorem](https://arxiv.org/abs/1308.1135);
* [Tame Decompositions and Collisions](https://arxiv.org/abs/1402.5945).

They do not claim to determine the completed scheme structure of
intersections of coefficient-image ideals, nor the result of projecting
away a coefficient.  Nilpotent path-incidence structure, integral contact
orders, and primary synchronization are consequently additional local
algebra, not contradictions to classical Ritt theory.

The conceptual bridge is:

\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{admissible-cover boundary}&\text{Ritt intersection}\\
\hline
\text{collision forest}&\text{relation 2-complex}\\
\text{target/source node contact}&\text{common-factor/move contact}\\
\text{Harris--Mumford node algebra}&\text{relative defect algebra}\\
\text{node-cycle character}&\text{synchronization/move coherence}.
\end{array}
\tag{8.1}
\]

The analogy is structural, not literal.  The admissible-cover ambient ring
has the uniform binomial presentation
\[
 s_{\epsilon j}^{e_{\epsilon j}}=q_\epsilon.
\]
No equally uniform Ritt defect presentation is presently known.  The
degree-thirty sectors show that one should expect a small family of
contact-graded complete-intersection blocks attached to commuting and braid
cells, rather than a single node equation.
