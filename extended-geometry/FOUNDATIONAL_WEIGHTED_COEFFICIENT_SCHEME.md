# The foundational weighted coefficient scheme

This note independently reconstructs the finite weighted ansatz described in
the supplied “Why This Counterexample, Step by Step” summary.  It uses only:

- source weights `(1,-1,-2)`;
- output weights `(-2,-1,1)`;
- component degree bounds `(7,6,4)`;
- linearity in `z`;
- the fixed linear part `(z,y,2x)`.

These conditions give exactly sixteen monomials.  The resulting
constant-Jacobian coefficient scheme is a single nonreduced point after the
effective two-dimensional diagonal gauge is fixed.

## 1. The sixteen-monomial ansatz

Put `u=xy` and `v=x^2z`.  Every map in the ansatz has

\[
 F=(x^{-2}A(u,v),x^{-1}B(u,v),xC(u,v)),
\]

where

\[
\begin{aligned}
A={}&v+A_{20}u^2+A_{11}uv+A_{30}u^3+A_{21}u^2v
       +A_{40}u^4+A_{31}u^3v,\\
B={}&u+B_{01}v+B_{20}u^2+B_{11}uv+B_{30}u^3+B_{21}u^2v,\\
C={}&2+C_{10}u+C_{01}v.
\end{aligned}                                           \tag{1}
\]

Thus the three components contain respectively `7,6,3` monomials.
The general
[weighted determinant lemma](WEIGHTED_INVARIANT_JACOBIAN_REDUCTION.md)
gives

\[
 \det JF=
 \det
 \begin{pmatrix}
 -2A&A_u&A_v\\
 -B&B_u&B_v\\
 C&C_u&C_v
 \end{pmatrix}.                                      \tag{2}
\]

The constant term of (2) is already `-2`.  Equating its other sixteen
`u,v` coefficients to zero defines the Keller ideal `I` in the thirteen
unknown coefficients of (1).

## 2. The effective diagonal gauge

Apply the diagonal source change

\[
 (x,y,z)\longmapsto(r x,s y,t z)
\]

and the unique diagonal target change restoring the linear part
`(z,y,2x)`.  The two coefficients

\[
 p=C_{10}=[x^2y]F_3,\qquad q=C_{01}=[x^3z]F_3
\]

transform by

\[
 p\longmapsto rs\,p,\qquad q\longmapsto r^2t\,q.
\]

On `pq!=0` choose `rs=-3/p` and `r^2t=-1/q`.  The remaining parameter `r`
is precisely the source-weight torus and acts trivially on all coefficients
in (1).  Hence the effective two-dimensional gauge has the unique normalized
chart

\[
 C_{10}=-3,\qquad C_{01}=-1.                         \tag{3}
\]

This is only the diagonal ansatz gauge.  It is not the quotient by arbitrary
polynomial or stable left--right equivalence.

## 3. Exact local algebra

In the chart (3), the Keller ideal has the following triangular Gröbner
presentation:

\[
\begin{aligned}
&(B_{01}-3)^2,\\
&12A_{20}-7B_{01}-27,\quad
 2A_{11}-B_{01}-3,\quad
 4A_{30}-9B_{01}-1,\\
&A_{21}-B_{01},\quad
 2A_{40}-3B_{01}+3,\quad
 2A_{31}-B_{01}+1,\\
&2B_{20}-11B_{01}+9,\quad
 B_{11}-3B_{01}+3,\\
&B_{30}-6B_{01}+9,\quad
 B_{21}-2B_{01}+3.
\end{aligned}                                        \tag{4}
\]

Consequently

\[
\boxed{\mathcal O_{\rm Keller,gauge}
\simeq\mathbb Q[\varepsilon]/(\varepsilon^2),\qquad
\varepsilon=B_{01}-3.}                               \tag{5}
\]

The unique closed point `epsilon=0` is the foundational polynomial.

## 4. The explicit infinitesimal deformation

Substitution `B_01=3+epsilon` in (4) gives

\[
 F_\varepsilon=F+\varepsilon H,\qquad\varepsilon^2=0,
\]

with `H_3=0` and

\[
\begin{aligned}
H_1={}&\frac7{12}y^2+\frac12xyz+\frac94xy^3
       +x^2y^2z+\frac32x^2y^4+\frac12x^3y^3z,\\
H_2={}&xz+\frac{11}{2}xy^2+3x^2yz+6x^2y^3+2x^3y^2z.
\end{aligned}                                        \tag{6}
\]

Direct differentiation proves

\[
 {d\over d\varepsilon}\bigg|_{\varepsilon=0}
 \det J(F+\varepsilon H)=0.
\]

Along the literal affine line `F+tH` one has

\[
 \det J(F+tH)=-2+t^2\Omega(x,y,z),                   \tag{7}
\]

where `Omega` is a nonzero polynomial recorded by the exact checker.
Thus the displayed tangent vector is obstructed at quadratic order along
that line.  The full Gröbner presentation (4), not merely (7), proves that no
second-order correction inside the normalized sixteen-monomial ansatz turns
it into a reduced curve.

An exact coefficient comparison also shows that `H` is **not** generated by
affine source and target vector fields:

\[
 H\ne DF\cdot V+W\circ F
\]

for affine-linear `V,W`.  This separates it from the affine left--right orbit.
Whether it lies in the orbit tangent generated by arbitrary polynomial
vector fields, or becomes trivial after stabilization, remains open.

## 5. The omitted boundary is concentrated at `p=q=0`

There are no Keller points on either punctured coordinate divisor.  Indeed,
the effective diagonal gauge sends every point with `p=0,q!=0` to
`(p,q)=(0,-1)` and every point with `q=0,p!=0` to `(-3,0)`.  Exact
coefficient comparison and Gröbner reduction give the unit ideal in both
normalized charts.  Thus, set-theoretically,

\[
 V(I)\cap V(pq)\subseteq V(p,q).                   \tag{8}
\]

This is a support-specific statement; it is not a general theorem about
weighted Keller maps.

### The two reduced components at the constant-`C` corner

The unsaturated boundary is not empty and is not merely a nilpotent artifact.
On the codimension-two locus

\[
 p=C_{10}=0,\qquad q=C_{01}=0,
\]

equation (2) becomes

\[
 \det JF=2(A_uB_v-A_vB_u).
\]

The ten coefficient equations have exactly two associated primes.

The first reduced component is

\[
 A=v+\alpha _2u^2+\alpha _3u^3+\alpha _4u^4,
 \qquad B=u,qquad C=2.                             \tag{9}
\]

It is an affine three-space of triangular automorphisms

\[
 F=(z+\alpha _2y^2+\alpha _3xy^3+\alpha _4x^2y^4,
 y,2x).
\]

The second reduced component is

\[
 A=v+\alpha _2u^2+\alpha _3u^3,
 \qquad B=u+\lambda A,\qquad C=2.                 \tag{10}
\]

This is another affine three-space.  Its maps are automorphisms because
`A=v+f(u)` and `B=u+lambda*A` recover `u=B-lambda*A` and then `v=A-f(u)`.
In the original coordinates they are

\[
 (x,y,z)\longmapsto
 \bigl(Z_0,\ y+\lambda xZ_0,\ 2x\bigr),\qquad
 Z_0=z+\alpha _2y^2+\alpha _3xy^3,
\]

so the inverse is polynomial in the displayed order.
The earlier affine line `(A,B)=(v,u+lambda*v)` is the slice
`alpha_2=alpha_3=0` of this larger component.

In coefficient coordinates, the two primes are

\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathfrak p_1={}&(B_{01},B_{20},B_{11},B_{30},B_{21},
 A_{11},A_{21},A_{31}),\\
\mathfrak p_2={}&(B_{21},B_{11},A_{31},A_{40},A_{21},A_{11},
 B_{20}-A_{20}B_{01},B_{30}-A_{30}B_{01}).
\end{aligned}                                      \tag{11}
\]

The redundant relation `A_30 B_20-A_20 B_30` may be included in a Gröbner
basis for `p_2`.  Both primes have affine dimension three.  Their reduced
intersection is the affine plane

\[
 A=v+\alpha _2u^2+\alpha _3u^3,\qquad B=u.         \tag{12}
\]

Exact `primdecGTZ` computation gives two primary components and no embedded
associated prime.  They are nonreduced: their projective degrees are `20`
and `30`, while the degrees of their radicals are `1` and `3`.  Thus (9) and
(10) exhaust the reduced constant-`C` boundary, and every point there is an
automorphism.

## 6. Scope and next boundary calculation

The dual-number result is a classification only inside (1) and after the
diagonal normalization (3).  The boundary decomposition is for the full
unnormalized ansatz (1).  Neither statement classifies all Keller maps of
degree profile `(7,6,4)`.

On the normalized chart (3), the further coordinates `s=-3u-v`, `t=-u` and
the products `P=C^2A`, `Q=CB` turn the complete equation into three
univariate weighted-Wronskian layers.  The
[weighted tangent-suspension note](WEIGHTED_TANGENT_SUSPENSION.md) proves
that this cascade generates the same dual-number ideal as (4).  This is the
preferred calculation on `pq!=0`.

The boundary calculation is complete at the level of primary components:
the punctured divisors are empty and `I+(C_10,C_01)` has precisely the two
components above.  The reduced global gluing can also be recovered without a
thirteen-variable Gröbner basis.

Write `alpha=-p/3` and `beta=-q`.  The effective diagonal-torus orbit of the
foundational point is

\[
\begin{aligned}
(A_{20},A_{11},A_{30},A_{21},A_{40},A_{31})
={}&(4\alpha^2/\beta,3\alpha,7\alpha^3/\beta,
3\alpha^2,3\alpha^4/\beta,\alpha^3),\\
(B_{01},B_{20},B_{11},B_{30},B_{21})
={}&(3\beta/\alpha,12\alpha,6\beta,9\alpha^2,
3\alpha\beta).
\end{aligned}                                      \tag{13}
\]

Eliminating `alpha,beta` after inverting their product gives a prime
degree-ten affine surface `T`.  Since the normalized reduced chart is a
point, `T` is the closure of the entire reduced `pq!=0` locus.  Its boundary
at `p=q=0` is the union of the two lines

\[
\begin{aligned}
L_A:&\quad A=v+\lambda u^2,\quad B=u,\\
L_B:&\quad A=v,\quad B=u+\lambda v.
\end{aligned}                                      \tag{14}
\]

The first line lies in both reduced boundary components; the second lies in
the component (10).  In particular,

\[
 T\cap V(\mathfrak p_1)=L_A,qquad
 T\cap V(\mathfrak p_2)=L_A\cup L_B.               \tag{15}
\]

Together with the empty one-sided charts, this proves the reduced global
decomposition

\[
 \boxed{V(I)_{\mathrm{red}}=T\cup V(\mathfrak p_1)
 \cup V(\mathfrak p_2).}                           \tag{16}
\]

What remains is purely scheme-theoretic: determine the nilpotent gluing of
the generically doubled toric component to the two nonreduced boundary
components along (14).  A bounded Singular attempt at the full global
standard basis still did not terminate; the radical and every reduced
attachment are nevertheless fixed by (13)--(16).

Exact tangent ranks measure the residual thickness.  The full
thirteen-variable scheme has tangent dimension `3` at the foundational open
point, versus reduced dimension `2` for `T`.  At generic points of the
boundary components (9) and (10), its tangent dimensions are respectively
`7` and `6`, versus reduced dimension `3`.  They jump to `8` along a generic
point of their reduced affine-plane intersection, remain `8` on a generic
point of `L_A`, and are `7` on a generic point of `L_B`.  Thus the missing
information is substantial nilpotent structure along already known reduced
strata, not evidence for another reduced component.
