\section{Consequences, provenance, and literature boundary}

Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is a realization theorem, not merely a source of
counterexamples.  Some immediate applications are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Every squarefree polynomial of degree at least three is, after one
      translation of its variable, the exact inverse polynomial of a full
      Keller fiber.
\item Every intersective polynomial of degree at least three transfers to a
      Hasse-failing Keller fiber without stabilization or auxiliary factors.
\item Any real signature, splitting field, or collection of local
      factorization patterns already carried by a finite \'etale algebra is
      carried by the corresponding Keller fiber.
\item Connected fibers require no additional geometric argument: start with
      an irreducible polynomial.
\end{enumerate}

The third item is deliberately a transfer statement.  It does not solve an
inverse Galois problem or assert that arbitrary independently prescribed
local algebras globalize; it says that every arithmetic realization which
already exists is preserved exactly by the Keller construction.

The realization is not unique.  Separate boundary calculations in the
accompanying research repository show that the same finite \'etale algebra
can occur in stably inequivalent weighted, cancellation, and quadratic-gauge
Keller maps.  That stable-classification problem is logically independent of
the present theorem and is best treated in a sequel, tentatively titled
\emph{One Fiber, Many Keller Maps}.

\paragraph{Literature boundary.}
A dated search completed on 24 July 2026 found no earlier statement that every
finite \'etale algebra of rank at least three is a full fiber of a polynomial
Keller map, nor the resulting rank classification.  This is necessarily a
qualified priority statement: bibliographic searches can miss unpublished
work, differently phrased results, and sources not indexed electronically.
The appropriate claim is therefore ``to our knowledge'', not an absolute
firstness assertion.  A reproducible search ledger is included with the
paper sources.

The closest results located have different scopes.  Gallagher gives explicit
Keller maps in every geometric degree and complete split rational fibers
\cite{Gallagher}; this realizes $K^N$, rather than an arbitrary finite
\'etale $K$-algebra.  Miranda--Neto proves radicality and maximal-ideal
decomposition properties for fibers already arising from a Keller map
\cite{Miranda}; this is not a prescribed-fiber realization theorem.
Lipton and Markakis study rational images and Hilbert irreducibility
\cite{LiptonMarkakis}, while Shaska studies graded Keller maps and arithmetic
thinness phenomena \cite{Shaska}.  None of these sources states the
finite-\'etale realization theorem proved here.

\paragraph{Verification.}
The companion checker
\[
 \texttt{scripts/verify\_finite\_etale\_keller\_fibers.py}
\]
verifies the source-chart and marked-line identities, two-sided
scheme-theoretic reconstruction in quotient rings, the exact scaling of the
explicit degree-five map, its determinant, the inverse polynomial identities,
and the fixed-map infinite family.  The universal proof is the calculation in
Proposition~\ref{prop:gauge}, not a bounded computer search.

\paragraph{Provenance.}
The recent three-dimensional counterexample was publicly attributed to
Alp\"oge and Fable.  Gallagher independently developed a weighted all-degree
viewpoint and an exact public atlas of complete rational fibers
\cite{Gallagher}.  The proof here instead uses the root-engineered
quadratic-gauge construction and the finite-\'etale realization theorem.
Berend and Bilu are credited for the classical intersective quintic.  The
rank-two exclusion uses the Galois theorem of Bass--Connell--Wright in its
arbitrary characteristic-zero ground-field form \cite{BCW}; Campbell, Razar,
and Wright are included for the historical development of that case
\cite{Campbell,Razar,Wright}.
