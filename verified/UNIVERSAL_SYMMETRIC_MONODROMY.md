# Universal symmetric monodromy of the linear pencil

This note isolates the monodromy statement used throughout the repository.
It is a theorem about an arbitrary polynomial, not a generic-polynomial
theorem and not a conclusion drawn from finite computations.

Let `k` be a field of characteristic zero, let `H in k[W]` have degree
`n>=2`, and set

\[
 E_H(W;s,t)=H(W)-sW+t.
\]

Write `G_geom` for the Galois group over `bar(k)(s,t)` and `G_arith` for the
Galois group over `k(s,t)`, both in their natural actions on the `n` roots.

> **Theorem (UM1).** For every such `H`,
> \[
>  G_{\rm geom}=G_{\rm arith}=S_n.
> \]
> Equivalently, the degree-`n` incidence cover over the complement of the
> discriminant has full symmetric geometric and arithmetic monodromy.  For
> `n>=3` its target-fixed deck group is trivial.

The point of the theorem is the quantifier **every**.  Monomials, Chebyshev
polynomials, decomposable polynomials, polynomials with affine symmetries, and
polynomials with colliding critical values may have exceptional *vertical
slices*.  None is exceptional for the two-parameter pencil.

## 1. The generic Morse-slice lemma

Work first over an algebraically closed characteristic-zero field `K`.  Put

\[
 f_\sigma(W)=H(W)-\sigma W.
\]

Recall that a degree-`n` polynomial is **Morse** if its `n-1` critical points
are simple and their critical values are pairwise distinct.

> **Generic Morse-slice lemma.** There is a finite set
> `Sigma_H subset K` such that `f_sigma` is Morse for every `sigma` outside
> `Sigma_H`.

Indeed, the repeated-root discriminant of the two-parameter pencil is
parametrized by

\[
 \nu:\mathbb A^1_r\longrightarrow\mathbb A^2_{s,t},\qquad
 r\longmapsto\bigl(H'(r),\ rH'(r)-H(r)\bigr).       \tag{1}
\]

The coordinate functions in (1) have degrees `n-1` and `n`.  The generic
degree of `nu` divides both degrees: by Lüroth's theorem a common rational
parameter for the image has degree dividing the pole order at infinity of
each coordinate.  Since `gcd(n-1,n)=1`, the map is birational onto its image.
It is also finite, because `r` is integral over `K[H'(r)]`.  Thus (1) is the
normalization of an irreducible affine plane curve and is injective away from
a finite set.

Now `f_sigma'(r)=H'(r)-sigma`.  Its roots fail to be simple only when

\[
 \sigma\in H'\bigl(V(H'')\bigr),                  \tag{2}
\]

a finite set because `H''` is nonzero in characteristic zero.  Two distinct
critical points `r_1,r_2` of `f_sigma` have the same critical value exactly
when

\[
 \nu(r_1)=\nu(r_2):
 \quad H'(r_1)=H'(r_2)=\sigma,
 \quad r_1H'(r_1)-H(r_1)=r_2H'(r_2)-H(r_2).       \tag{3}
\]

The noninjectivity locus of the finite birational normalization (1) is
finite.  Excluding its finitely many `s`-coordinates together with (2) proves
the lemma.  Notice that this proves the assertion for each fixed `H`; no
variation of the coefficients of `H` is involved.

## 2. Classical proof of full monodromy

Choose `sigma outside Sigma_H` by the generic Morse-slice lemma.  The
classical Morse-polynomial theorem says
that

\[
 \operatorname{Gal}\bigl(f_\sigma(W)-T/K(T)\bigr)=S_n.                 \tag{4}
\]

One standard reference is Serre, *Topics in Galois Theory*, Section 4.4,
especially Theorem 4.4.5.  Its branch-cycle proof is short: the cover is
connected; every finite branch point is simple and hence has transposition
inertia; the finite inertia groups generate; and a transitive permutation
group generated by transpositions is `S_n`.

The substitution `T=-t` identifies (4) with the restriction of `E_H=0` to
the vertical line `s=sigma`.  After deleting its branch points, this line maps
to the discriminant complement; functoriality of the fundamental-group action
makes its monodromy a subgroup of the ambient two-parameter monodromy.
Consequently

\[
 S_n\ \leq\ G_{\rm geom}\ \leq\ S_n,
\]

so `G_geom=S_n`.  This argument is algebraic over `bar(k)`: apply the lemma
and the Morse-polynomial theorem with `K=bar(k)`.  Finally

\[
 G_{\rm geom}\triangleleft G_{\rm arith}\leq S_n,
\]

which forces `G_arith=S_n` as well.

For completeness, `E_H` is geometrically irreducible even before the Morse
argument.  It has degree one in `t`; in a factorization in `K[s,W,t]`, one
factor has `t`-degree zero, and comparison of the coefficient of `t` makes
that factor a unit.  Gauss's lemma then gives irreducibility over `K(s,t)`.
This is the connectedness used by the two-parameter monodromy action.

An alternative algebraic proof stays on the parameter plane.  The curve (1)
is irreducible and generically parametrizes one double root, so its generic
inertia is a transposition.  The normal subgroup generated by all divisorial
inertia leaves a cover unramified in codimension one; Zariski--Nagata purity
extends it to a finite etale cover of `A^2`, which is trivial in
characteristic zero.  Hence the full group is generated transitively by
transpositions.  This is useful as a scheme-theoretic cross-check, but the
vertical Morse argument above is the classical one-line reduction.

## 3. Why the standard exceptional families are not exceptions

The distinction to keep in view is

\[
 \text{monodromy of one slice }H(W)-s_0W+t
 \quad\hbox{versus}\quad
 \text{monodromy over }k(s,t).                     \tag{5}
\]

Specialization can lower monodromy.  The lemma says that only finitely many
tilts can fail the Morse criterion, while any one Morse tilt already detects
the full group of the two-parameter family.

| Polynomial phenomenon | Exceptional slice | What the moving parameter does |
|---|---|---|
| Monomial | For `H=W^n`, the slice `s=0` has cyclic geometric monodromy (smaller than `S_n` when `n>=3`). | If `sigma!=0`, the critical points satisfy `r^(n-1)=sigma/n`, and their critical values are `-(n-1)sigma*r/n`; they are simple and distinct. Thus every nonzero tilt is Morse. |
| Chebyshev | For `H=T_n`, the slice `s=0` has dihedral monodromy. Its critical values lie in `{+1,-1}` and collide when `n>=4`; the dihedral group is smaller than `S_n` when `n>=4`. | All but finitely many `sigma` make `T_n-sigma W` Morse, so those vertical slices and the full pencil have monodromy `S_n`. |
| Decomposable `H=A(B(W))` | The slice `s=0` is imprimitive. | All but finitely many linear tilts have monodromy `S_n`, hence are primitive and indecomposable. The decomposition of `H` is not a decomposition of the pencil over `k(s)`. |
| Large affine symmetry | A centered power, or another symmetric special slice, can have visible source automorphisms. | Pencil-compatible symmetries generally move `(s,t)` and are not target-fixed deck transformations. Full monodromy is unchanged. |
| Repeated or multiple critical values | At an exceptional `s_0`, (1) may have a cusp or several normalization points over one discriminant point. | These give only finitely many bad `s`-values. Away from them, all ramification in the vertical slice is simple with distinct branch values. |

Adding a linear polynomial to `H` causes no issue at all: if
`H=H_0+aW+b`, then translating the parameters by `s -> s-a` and `t -> t+b`
identifies the two pencils.

For decompositions, the familiar equivalence is that the monodromy of a
polynomial cover is imprimitive exactly when the polynomial decomposes
nontrivially.  This follows from the block/intermediate-field correspondence,
Lüroth's theorem, and total ramification at infinity; a convenient reference
is Zieve--Müller, *On Ritt's polynomial decomposition theorems*, Section 2.
Thus the `S_n` conclusion is fully compatible with Ritt theory: it says that
generic linear tilt leaves every proper Ritt locus.

## 4. Deck transformations and affine symmetries

For a connected degree-`n` cover, target-fixed deck transformations form the
centralizer of the monodromy action on a geometric fiber.  In the natural
action of `S_n`, this centralizer is trivial for `n>=3`.  Therefore the
generic incidence cover has no nonidentity target-fixed deck transformation.

This does not say that the *family* has no automorphisms covering a change of
base coordinates.  For example, affine symmetries of a centered monomial may
act simultaneously on `W,s,t`.  Those are pencil self-equivalences, not deck
transformations over the fixed `(s,t)`-plane, and they do not contradict
UM1.

## 5. Standard references and repository role

- Jean-Pierre Serre, [*Topics in Galois Theory*, Section 4.4, Theorem
  4.4.5](https://indico.math.cnrs.fr/event/11410/attachments/4760/7351/SerreTopicsGaloisTheory.pdf): a Morse polynomial has Galois/monodromy group
  `S_n` over the value-function field; the proof uses finite inertia and
  elementary permutation-group arguments.
- Michael E. Zieve and Peter Müller, [*On Ritt's polynomial decomposition
  theorems*, Section 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/0807.3578): polynomial
  decomposition, intermediate fields, and monodromy imprimitivity.
- [Stacks Project, Lemma 58.21.4](https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0BMB):
  purity of the branch locus, used only for the alternative parameter-plane
  proof.
- SGA 1, [Expose XII, Theorem 5.1 and Corollary
  5.2](https://firmaprim.github.io/sga/sga-1/): Riemann existence comparison,
  one standard route to the characteristic-zero simple-connectedness input
  in the alternative proof.

The weighted construction uses this theorem after the purely transcendental
change of parameters `s=BC`, `t=cAC^2`.  Symbolic and finite-field scripts in
the repository test examples and reductions; they are not evidence for the
universal quantifier in UM1.  That quantifier is supplied by the lemma and
the classical Morse-polynomial theorem above.
