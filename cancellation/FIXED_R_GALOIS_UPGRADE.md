# Fixed-row upgrade to alternating and symmetric groups

This note isolates the two tasks left after fixed-row transitivity.  Throughout,
fix `r>=1`, put

\[
 d=mr,
 \qquad
 B_{d,r}(x)=\sum_{j=0}^d\binom{r+j}{r}x^j,
\]

and **assume** that `B_(d,r)` is irreducible.  Thus its Galois group `G` is a
transitive subgroup of `S_d`.  Irreducibility, discriminants, and their
exceptional sets are not reconsidered here.

The fixed-row Newton argument supplies, for density-one many `m`, a prime
`p | d+1` and an element of order `p` in `G`.  What it does not yet supply is

1. an element supported on exactly one `p`-cycle; and
2. primitivity of `G`.

Both steps are necessary.  If they hold with `p<=d-3`, Jordan's theorem gives
`A_d subseteq G`; the existing nonsquare-discriminant result then gives
`G=S_d` on a density-one set.

## 1. The block obstruction after a pure cycle

The numerical relation `p | d+1` makes the block problem substantially
smaller than a general primitivity problem.

### Lemma 1 (block-size cutoff)

Let `G<=S_d` be transitive and contain a `p`-cycle, where `p` is prime and
`p` does not divide `d`.  In every nontrivial block system, the size `c` of a
block satisfies

\[
 c>p.                                                     \tag{1}
\]

#### Proof

Let the `p`-cycle be `sigma`.  Its induced permutation on the blocks has
order `1` or `p`.  If it has order `p`, it cycles `p` blocks.  Every point in
those blocks moves to a different block, so the support of `sigma` has size
at least `pc`.  Since its support has size `p`, this forces `c=1`, contrary
to nontriviality.  Hence `sigma` fixes every block setwise.  Its support is
then contained in one block, so `c>=p`.  Finally `c | d` and `p` does not
divide `d`, whence `c!=p`.  This proves (1).

In the Newton setting `d=-1 mod p`, so the coprimality hypothesis is
automatic.  Consequently only divisors

\[
 c\mid d,\qquad p<c\le d/2                             \tag{2}
\]

can survive.  If `lambda(d)` is the least prime divisor of `d`, the largest
proper divisor is `d/lambda(d)`.  Thus a pure `p`-cycle proves primitivity as
soon as

\[
 p\ge \frac d{\lambda(d)}.                              \tag{3}
\]

Writing `d+1=pa_0`, a convenient sufficient condition for (3) is
`a_0<=lambda(d)`.

### A closed sparse subfamily

If `d+1=2p`, then an order-`p` permutation in degree `d=2p-1` can contain
only one `p`-cycle.  It is therefore already a pure `p`-cycle.  Also
`p>d/2`, so Lemma 1 proves primitivity.  Hence:

> Whenever the existing Newton edge and irreducibility hold for a parameter
> with `mr+1=2p` and `p<=mr-3`, the Galois group contains `A_(mr)`; a nonsquare
> discriminant makes it `S_(mr)`.

This is a useful consistency check and an actual completion on the
cofactor-two subfamily, but it is too sparse to imply a density-one theorem.
The density-one Newton prime can have unbounded cofactor `a_0`.

## 2. What local support really asks for

Let

\[
 a=d+1=pa_0,
 \qquad p\parallel a,
 \qquad p>r,
\]

and use the reciprocal numerator `W_(h,r)` from
[the Newton note](FIXED_R_NEWTON_RAMIFICATION.md).  Modulo `p`,

\[
 W_{h,r}(x)\equiv x^r(x^{a_0}-1)^p.                    \tag{4}
\]

At every nontrivial `a_0`-th root `zeta`, the proved Newton edge is

\[
 (0,1)\longrightarrow(p,0).                            \tag{5}
\]

Over the maximal unramified extension of `Q_p`, (5) gives a `p`-root
cluster above each `zeta`.  Inertia fixes the residue root, so an order-`p`
inertia element acts on the union of these clusters with cycle type
`p^t 1^f`.  The missing assertion is that the wild inertia image contains a
coordinate element: nontrivial on one cluster and trivial on every other
cluster.

This formulation exposes two distinct separations.

1. **Residue-orbit separation.**  Over `Q_p`, Frobenius groups the roots
   `zeta` according to the irreducible factors of `x^(a_0)-1` in `F_p[x]`.
   A decomposition group distinguishes those factors, not automatically the
   individual geometric roots inside a factor of degree greater than one.
2. **Wild-extension separation.**  Even two linear residue clusters may
   induce the same degree-`p` quotient of wild inertia.  Their action can be
   diagonal, so merely factoring `x^(a_0)-1` over `F_p` does not isolate a
   cycle.

A precise sufficient local lemma would be the following.

### Target L (local separation)

For one nontrivial residue root `zeta`, the wild degree-`p` subextension
generated by its cluster is not contained in the compositum of the wild
subextensions generated by the other clusters.

The kernel of the action on the other clusters then acts nontrivially on the
`zeta` cluster.  An order-`p` element of this kernel is a single `p`-cycle.

Three concrete ways to attack Target L are:

- compute the ramification polygons beyond the common first edge (5) and
  find a cluster with a unique higher break;
- after adjoining `mu_p`, compare the Kummer classes attached to the
  clusters and prove one is independent of the others; or
- force a linear residue cluster (an `a_0`-th root in `F_p`) and distinguish
  its local factor by a resolvent or different exponent.

The common slope `1/p` alone cannot do this.  Any proposed local proof must
identify the extra invariant that prevents a diagonal wild-inertia image.

## 3. Frobenius support as an alternative

Let `q` be an auxiliary prime at which the polynomial is squarefree.  If its
factor degrees modulo `q` contain exactly one `p`, and every other degree is
coprime to `p`, Dedekind's theorem gives an element `tau in G` of that cycle
type.  Raising `tau` to the least common multiple of the other cycle lengths
kills all other cycles and leaves a single `p`-cycle.

This route bypasses the local diagonal problem completely.  Its missing
uniform input is:

### Target F (forced Frobenius type)

For density-one many fixed-row parameters carrying a Newton prime `p`,
produce an explicit good auxiliary prime `q` for which the factorization of
`M_(m,r)` modulo `q` has exactly one degree-`p` factor and no other factor
degree divisible by `p`.

Chebotarev is not by itself a proof of Target F: it produces this
factorization only after one already knows that `G` contains the desired
cycle type.  A proof has to force the factorization arithmetically, average
over `m`, or obtain the cycle by another method first.

## 4. Exact cycle-type obstruction to blocks

Auxiliary factorizations can also attack primitivity without relying on a
single `(d-1,1)` type.  The following criterion is convenient for combining
several modular types.

### Lemma 2 (wreath compatibility)

Let `lambda_1,...,lambda_s` be a cycle type in degree `d`, and let `c | d`.
This type occurs in the imprimitive action with block size `c` if and only if
the cycles can be partitioned into groups `S_j` such that, for each group,
there is an integer `ell_j` satisfying

\[
 ell_j\mid lambda_i\quad(i\in S_j),
 \qquad
 \sum_{i\in S_j}\frac{\lambda_i}{\ell_j}=c.            \tag{6}
\]

#### Proof

For an orbit of `ell_j` blocks, the `ell_j`-th power of the permutation acts
on one block.  A cycle of length `mu` in that block lifts to a point cycle of
length `ell_j mu`; all point cycles over that block orbit therefore satisfy
(6).  Conversely, data satisfying (6) constructs the required permutation
on that orbit of blocks.  Taking the disjoint union over the groups proves
the equivalence.

For a transitive Galois group, every observed unramified Frobenius type must
be compatible with every genuine block size.  Intersecting the compatible
divisors over several auxiliary primes can therefore prove primitivity.
The familiar `(d-1,1)` certificate is the strongest one-prime special case:
it eliminates every proper block size.

The density-one version is again an arithmetic distribution problem:

### Target B (uniform block sieve)

For every divisor `c` in (2), force at least one good modular factorization
whose cycle type fails (6), using a number of auxiliary primes that is
uniform enough to preserve a density-one exceptional estimate.

## 5. Consecutive-coefficient structure

The monic cancellation polynomial is

\[
 M_{m,r}(x)=\sum_{j=0}^{d}(-1)^j
 \binom{d+r+1}{j}x^{d-j}.
\]

Put `N=d+r+1`.  Completing the binomial expansion gives the exact identity

\[
 x^{r+1}M_{m,r}(x)
 =(x-1)^N-(-1)^N\sum_{k=0}^r(-1)^k\binom Nk x^k.        \tag{7}
\]

The rightmost polynomial has fixed degree `r`.  Identity (7) is the most
promising place to use the consecutive coefficients against imprimitive
wreath products: modulo a fixed auxiliary prime, Lucas congruences control
the short polynomial while Frobenius controls large powers of `(x-1)`.

What is not yet proved is a theorem turning (7) into either Target F or
Target B.  In particular, compositional indecomposability of the displayed
polynomial would not by itself settle the arithmetic block question: blocks
for the Galois action correspond to intermediate fields of `Q(alpha)/Q`, not
merely to a polynomial composition.

## 6. Modular experiment

The separate script

```bash
.venv/bin/python scripts/experiment_fixed_r_modular_factorization.py
```

scans a bounded set of rows and auxiliary primes.  It records a Target-F
factorization when found and applies the exact-cover form of Lemma 2 to every
proper divisor of `d`.  Transitivity is treated as an input, exactly as in
this note.

For the larger reproducible run

```bash
.venv/bin/python scripts/experiment_fixed_r_modular_factorization.py \
  --rows 1,2,3,4 --max-m 40 --prime-bound 200
```

there are `69` parameters among `148` tested degrees with a prime satisfying
the elementary conditions `p || mr+1`, `p>r`, and `p<=mr-3`.  (The
experiment does not test the BFLT bad-pair condition.)  The scanned
Frobenius types eliminate every proper block size in all `69` cases, and
isolate one of the candidate primes in `64` cases.  One
support miss is `(m,r)=(17,1)`: its square discriminant and known group
`A_17` forbid a transposition, while the only eligible Newton prime is `2`.
The remaining four misses are search-bound misses in row `r=3`, not proved
exceptions.

These data favor both targets, but their role is diagnostic.  They identify
small auxiliary primes and exact cycle patterns from which a uniform
congruence argument might be reverse-engineered; they do not replace such an
argument.

## 7. Recommended proof order

1. Prove Target L for at least one checkable class of cofactors `a_0`; the
   cofactor-two case is already automatic, and `a_0=3` is the first genuine
   test of diagonal versus independent wild inertia.
2. In parallel, use (7) to seek an averaged Target-F congruence.  A single
   forced factorization gives the pure cycle without local field comparison.
3. Once a pure cycle is available, discard all block sizes using Lemma 1.
   Apply modular Lemma 2 only to the surviving divisors `c>p`, rather than
   trying to prove primitivity from scratch.
4. Apply Jordan only after both support and blocks have been certified; then
   invoke the existing discriminant theorem to distinguish `A_d` from
   `S_d`.

The key conceptual point is that support and primitivity are separate.  The
Newton edge controls ramification order, local separation controls support,
and modular or structural information controls blocks.
