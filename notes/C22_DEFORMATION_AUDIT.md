# Independent deformation-theoretic audit of C22

This note reproves the all-`k` transfer-block theorem without taking an
invariant ring, using a Reynolds operator, or invoking the regular
representation of the symmetric group. The replacement is the deformation
theory of the cusp normalization and the divided-power symmetric product of a
split ribbon.

Throughout, `K` is a characteristic-zero field and

\[
 S(Z)=Z^k+s_1Z^{k-1}+\cdots+s_k.
\]

## 1. The one-root normalization deformation

Write the cusp and its normalization as

\[
 C=\operatorname{Spec}K[u,v]/(u^2-v^3),\qquad
 \nu:\mathbb A^1_q\longrightarrow C,\quad q\longmapsto(q^3,q^2).
\]

Fixing the root position, a monic deformation has a unique transverse
coordinate `epsilon`. Direct coefficient comparison gives

\[
 v=q^2+\epsilon,qquad
 u=q^3+\frac32\epsilon q,qquad \epsilon^2=0.                    \tag{1}
\]

Indeed, after centering the root write
`v=q^2+d` and `u=q^3+a q^2+bq+c`. The coefficients of degrees five, four,
and three give `a=0`, `b=3d/2`, and `c=0`; the degree-two coefficient then
gives `d^2=0`. Thus

\[
 \operatorname{Def}_{\nu}^{\mathrm{tr}}
       =\operatorname{Spf}K[[\epsilon]]/(\epsilon^2).             \tag{2}
\]

This is a deformation calculation for the normalization map, not a root
quotient. It also explains why each separated root contributes length two.

## 2. The conductor ribbon and its symmetric-product moduli problem

Let

\[
 \mathscr R=\operatorname{Spec}K[z,\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)
 \longrightarrow \mathbb A^1_z                                  \tag{3}
\]

be the split ribbon whose vertical direction is (1). Define

\[
 \mathfrak B_k=\Gamma^k(\mathscr R),
 \qquad \mathcal S_k=\Gamma^k(\mathbb A^1)
                 =\operatorname{Spec}K[s_1,\ldots,s_k],
                                                                    \tag{4}
\]

where `Gamma^k` denotes the divided-power symmetric product. Over a
`Q`-algebra it agrees with the ordinary symmetric product, but (4) is defined
by multiplicative polynomial laws; no group quotient enters the argument.

A `T`-point of `mathfrak B_k` over the divisor `D=V(S)` is an unordered
degree-`k` collection of infinitesimal conductor modifications (1), including
their confluent limits when points of `D` collide. This is the promised
moduli interpretation of the Boolean generators.

## 3. The ribbon straightening lemma

Let

\[
 \mathcal E_k=\mathcal O_{\mathcal S_k}[z]/(S)                    \tag{5}
\]

be the tautological rank-`k` algebra. For
`p:mathfrak B_k->mathcal S_k`, filter `p_*O_(mathfrak B_k)` by the number of
vertical inputs in the universal degree-`k` polynomial law. Denote this finite
filtration by `F^d`.

**Ribbon straightening lemma.** The morphism `p` is finite flat and

\[
 \operatorname{rank}_{\mathcal O_{\mathcal S_k}}F^d/F^{d+1}={k\choose d},
 \qquad 0\le d\le k.                                             \tag{6}
\]

The positive part is generated by `F^1/F^2`; after restriction to the
maximally collided divisor `S=Z^k`, the filtration `F` is the maximal-ideal
filtration.

### Proof without invariant theory

Polarize the universal norm law in exactly `d` vertical inputs. Polynomial
division by the monic equation `S(z)=0` reduces every input to a combination
of `1,z,...,z^(k-1)`. When two support points coalesce, replace ordinary
evaluation by confluent evaluation, using the divided difference

\[
 \partial f(x,y)=\frac{f(x)-f(y)}{x-y}.                           \tag{7}
\]

The product rule and the recursive higher divided difference

\[
 \partial(fg)=f(x)\partial g+g(y)\partial f,
 \qquad
 f[x_0,\ldots,x_m]
 =\frac{f[x_0,\ldots,x_{m-1}]-f[x_1,\ldots,x_m]}{x_0-x_m}       \tag{8}
\]

give a terminating straightening rule. Its overlap ambiguities agree because
higher divided differences are symmetric in their nodes, including their
confluent values. In vertical degree `d` the normal symbols are indexed by
strictly increasing `d`-tuples

\[
 0\le a_1<\cdots<a_d<k.                                         \tag{9}
\]

For a support subset `I`, the symbol indexed by `(a_1,...,a_d)` evaluates as

\[
 \frac{\det(c_j^{a_i})_{i,j\in I}}
      {\prod_{i<j,\ i,j\in I}(c_j-c_i)}.                         \tag{10}
\]

The quotient is a Schur polynomial: exact division, not inversion of a
discriminant. Repeated inputs do not vanish; (7) straightens them to
combinations of the symbols (9). This is why the collided multiplication is
not an exterior algebra even though the ranks are exterior-power ranks.

The straightening is monic, and the symmetry of higher divided differences
resolves every overlap, so the normal-form lemma already gives uniqueness.
As a separate independence check, specialize temporarily to
`S=prod_(i=1)^k(Z-c_i)` with algebraically independent distinct `c_i`.
Evaluation of (9) on the `d`-element support subsets is the normalized `d`-th
compound matrix of the Vandermonde matrix `(c_j^a)`: each column is divided
by its internal Vandermonde as in (10). Its determinant is

\[
 \det(V)^{\binom{k-2}{d-1}}\ne0,                                 \tag{11}
\]

with exponent zero at `d=0,k`. Indeed the ordinary compound determinant has
exponent `binom(k-1,d-1)`, while the product of the internal Vandermondes has
exponent `binom(k-2,d-2)`. Thus the normal forms have exactly the asserted
generic specialization and form a basis of `F^d/F^(d+1)`.

The direct sum of the ranks in (6) is `2^k`. Successive extensions of finite
free modules are finite free, proving that `p` is finite flat of rank `2^k`.
Polarization expresses every normal symbol as a product of degree-one
vertical coefficients, proving the generation statement. At `S=Z^k` the
only residue field element of degree zero is a scalar, so the positive
filtration is exactly the maximal ideal and (6) is its Hilbert function.
This proves the lemma. `square`

The argument is a confluent-Vandermonde calculation in a divided-power
moduli problem. It does not use exactness of invariants, reflection-group
freeness, or the coinvariant algebra.

## 4. The norm map to the square/cube factorization fiber

The one-root deformation (1) is multiplicative. Applying the universal norm
of the degree-`k` cycle defines monic polynomials

\[
\begin{aligned}
 V(Z)&=\operatorname{Nm}\big((Z-z)^2+\epsilon\big),\\
 U(Z)&=\operatorname{Nm}\big((Z-z)^3+\tfrac32\epsilon(Z-z)\big).
\end{aligned}                                                     \tag{12}
\]

Equation (1) gives `U^2=V^3`, so (12) defines a morphism of formal schemes

\[
                 \widehat{\mathfrak B}_k\longrightarrow Z_k.     \tag{13}
\]

Here completion is along the zero vertical section. Formula (12) is
intrinsic: it uses the norm polynomial law of the universal divisor and
therefore remains defined at collisions.

## 5. Formal comparison: no missing deformations

We prove that (13) is an isomorphism directly from the cusp normalization.
Write a deformation in `Z_k` uniquely as

\[
 V=S^2+A,qquad \deg A<k,                                        \tag{14}
\]

using the highest `k` coefficients to define `S`. Monic Weierstrass division
then eliminates the `3k` nonleading coefficients of `U` successively from
the coefficients of degrees `6k-1,...,3k` in `U^2=V^3`.

Let `I` be the ideal generated by the coefficients of `A`. In the Laurent
series ring at infinity, the unique square root congruent to `S^3` is

\[
 U=\left[S^3\left(1+\frac{A}{S^2}\right)^{3/2}\right]_+ ,
                                                                    \tag{15}
\]

and the remaining equations say that the negative Laurent part vanishes.
In `gr_I` the degree-`d` leading obstruction is therefore

\[
 \operatorname{pp}_{-}\left(
   {3/2\choose d}A^dS^{3-2d}\right).                              \tag{16}
\]

Polarize (16) at the roots of `S`. A repeated support index is a principal
part, and (16), whose binomial coefficient is nonzero in characteristic
zero, rewrites it in terms of smaller pole order. Exact divided differences
(7)--(8) carry out the same rewrite when roots coalesce. The only irreducible
degree-`d` terms are the distinct-support Schur symbols (9); for `d>k` there
are none. Symmetry of higher divided differences resolves the overlap
ambiguities, exactly as in the ribbon straightening lemma.

Thus `gr_I O_(Z_k)` is generated over `K[s_1,...,s_k]` by at most

\[
                       \sum_d{k\choose d}=2^k.                   \tag{17}
\]

Because `I^(k+1)/I^(k+2)=0`, completeness and separatedness give
`I^(k+1)=0`; lifting the normal symbols consequently generates the completed
coordinate algebra itself, not merely its associated graded.

Conversely, the coefficients of the norms (12) generate the ribbon algebra.
It is enough to check vertical degree one, because the straightening lemma
also says that the positive algebra is generated there. If
`theta_a=sum_i r_i^a epsilon_i`, the degree-one part of `V` is

\[
       \delta V=\sum_i\epsilon_i
                    \left(\frac{S(Z)}{Z-r_i}\right)^2.           \tag{18}
\]

For `0<=a<k`, the coefficient of `Z^(2k-2-a)` in (18) is triangular in
`theta_0,...,theta_a`, with diagonal coefficient `a+1`. Over a
characteristic-zero base these coefficients recover all `theta_a`. Products
and the divided-difference straightening then recover every normal symbol
(9). Hence (13) is a closed immersion, or equivalently its map on complete
coordinate rings is surjective.

Put `R_Z=O_(Z_k)` and `R_B=O_(widehat(mathfrak B)_k)`. The closed immersion
just proved is a surjection `R_Z -> R_B`. Here `R_Z` needs at most `2^k`
generators over the base by (17), while `R_B` is free of rank `2^k` by the
ribbon straightening lemma. Localize at any point of the base. Since `R_B`
is free, the surjection splits as a map of modules:
`R_Z ~= R_B direct_sum ker`. Additivity of minimal numbers of generators and
Nakayama's lemma force the kernel to vanish. Hence

\[
                         \widehat{\mathfrak B}_k\simeq Z_k.       \tag{19}
\]

This proves simultaneously that no additional embedded or collision
nilpotents occur. Notice that the upper bound (17), not a generic-rank
argument alone, is what excludes torsion supported on the discriminant.

## 6. Consequences

From (6) and (19):

1. `Z_k -> A^k_S` is finite flat of rank `2^k`.
2. At `S=Z^k`,

   \[
   \dim_K\mathfrak m^d/\mathfrak m^{d+1}={k\choose d},
   \qquad \operatorname{Hilb}(t)=(1+t)^k.                         \tag{20}
   \]

3. Over a divisor with disjoint support, the norm and Hensel factorization
   split (19) as the tensor product of the local one-root deformations (2).
4. At a collision, divided differences give the flat confluent limit. They
   preserve the filtration ranks but change multiplication, explaining the
   non-Gorenstein fibers already visible for `k=2`.

The affine-difference equality `Z_k^aff=Z_k` remains independently covered
by the differential Wronskian identities in C22; it does not use either the
invariant-theoretic proof or the deformation-theoretic argument above.
